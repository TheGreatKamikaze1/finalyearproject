ABSTRACT
Mental health issues and problems among university students are a growing worldwide health issue, as the incidence of stress, anxiety, and even depression impacts academic success, social networks, and well-being of university students. In Nigeria, the situation is further aggravated by the lack of accessibility to the professional mental health services, social stigma, and insufficient awareness. This study will give the design and development of Wellnest, a cross-platform mental wellness mobile application that was created to help students track and enhance their emotional health utilizing digital technology.
 In the study, the Design Thinking approach was used to define the needs of the users by empathy, problem definition, ideation, prototyping, and testing. Thereafter, after the requirement validation, Agile development model was adopted in such a way that flexibility, continuous feedback, and improvement in the development process were guaranteed. The system was also user-friendly and therefore it was designed in a user-centered manner, focusing on usability, accessibility, and simplicity. 
The major characteristics of the application are daily moods, activity tags, mood history, personal insights, simple mental wellness, and user authentication. The frontend part was created based on cross platform mobile framework in order to be compatible with both Android and iOS and the backend services and APIs were integrated to process user data safely and efficiently. The application will offer students a confidential and personalized, as well as supportive online space to reflect on themselves and track their emotions. Allowing stable mood tracking and trend detection, Wellnest motivates an individual to be mindful of the mental health trends early and maintain proactive control of well-being. 
The outcomes of the development process prove that the combination of the principles of the user-centered design and the application of Agile methodologies increases the usability and flexibility of the system. This study will be important to the discipline of Human-Computer Interaction and mobile health technology because it will show how intelligently designed digital responses can be utilized to solve the problem of mental wellness among institutions of higher learning. The Wellnest application is a viable, scalable, and technology-intensive strategy to assist student mental health in a limited resource setting.

CHAPTER ONE
INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background to the Study
Mental health issues are a significant global challenge, affecting people of all ages, genders, and socioeconomic backgrounds. Shetty (2025) highlighted that more than a billion people worldwide suffer from some form of mental health disorder, with anxiety and depression being the most common. Despite their high prevalence, access to treatment varies greatly: less than 10% of those affected in low-income countries receive care compared to over 50% in high-income countries. It was estimated that about 970 million people experienced mental health conditions in 2019, emphasizing the worldwide impact of these disorders.
Young people, especially university students, are among the most affected groups. Centre for Transforming Access and Student Outcomes [TASO] (2023) reported that in the UK, for example, the share of undergraduates reporting mental health difficulties almost tripled between the academic years 2016/17 and 2022/23, rising from 6% to 16%. Also, financial stress has been a major contributor, with more students thinking about dropping out because of money problems, increasing from 3.5% in 2022 to 8% in 2023. 
Similarly, in Nigeria, mental health challenges among university students are significant. Orok et al (2023) indicate that 58% of students experience depression with varying severity from mild to severe. Davis (2025) pointed out that the contributing factors include academic pressure, social isolation, peer conflicts, social media influence, and family-related stress.
Design Thinking has been applied effectively in creating user-centered digital mental health tools. Scholten and Granic (2019) developed interactive games like MindLight for youth by employing empathy-driven participatory design, multidisciplinary ideation, and iterative prototyping. Their method placed youth directly into the design process to meet motivational and emotional needs usually neglected in conventional interventions. Similarly, Sam, Richardson, and Currie (2022) merged Design Thinking with Indigenous storytelling to co-create an e-mental health screening app for Indigenous youth. Participants used Design Circles, persona development, and usability feedback to make sure it was culturally relevant and responsive. Bernaerts et al. (2025) took this further in the SUPER project by customizing mental health apps for several countries through the five stages of Design Thinking: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test, with strong emphasis on stakeholder participation, cultural adaptation, and iterative refinement.
Although these achievements have been made, there are still significant gaps. Most studies addressed youth or adult populations rather than specifically university students, and very few actively engaged this cohort as co-designers. There is a dearth of research in African contexts, plus the long-term engagement with, adoption of, and sustained effectiveness of these apps has not been assessed. Existing interventions may thus be irrelevant or unusable, or poorly culturally fit for the Nigerian university student population.
To address these gaps, this brings to light the imperative for studies that use Design Thinking in developing mental wellness apps specifically for university students, with their active participation in the design process while assessing usability and long-term impact. This project fills this niche by targeting Nigerian university students towards developing a culturally relevant, user-centered, effective digital tool that would cater to their mental health, academic performance, as well as general well-being.

Thus, this project is directed at the creation of a mental wellness app for university students to fill the gap of accessible user-centered mental health support in Nigerian universities. The research aims to comprehend the mental health difficulties faced by students and bring forth a tool that could enhance their psychological well-being, academic performance, and general quality of life.

The state of an individual’s mental health is paramount, and it determines how actively they participate and involve themselves in various activities in life. In addition, most wellness solutions fail to address their specific contexts and needs. Common issues include poor usability, irrelevant content, and a lack of emotional connection between the user and the application. The major problem, therefore, is the lack of user-centered, accessible, and engaging mental wellness platforms tailored for university students. 
In response to these challenges, this study aims to address the problem by designing and developing a functional, intuitive, and secure mental wellness application specifically tailored to enhance students’ emotional well-being, promote self-care practices, and provide accessible psychological support tools. The application aims to enhance the overall mental health support experience for users by incorporating features such as intuitive navigation, daily mood tracking, guided journaling, personalized wellness recommendations, user authentication, and secure data protection measures.

1.6 Limitations of the study
The study aims to create an empathetic, effective, and functional mobile application for a mental wellness application for university students; it is important to acknowledge certain limitations that may affect its scope and overall impact, including:
Firstly, the application will be developed exclusively for the Android platform. Due to time and resource constraints, the scope of the project does not include iOS development, which limits its reach to a portion of the mobile user market. Consequently, users of IOS devices will not have access to the application during the development phase.
In addition, during the empathize stage of the design thinking approach, many students may be reluctant to openly discuss their true mental health struggles due to the stigma associated with mental illness, fear of judgment, or concerns about privacy/confidentiality (especially if the research is affiliated with the university). This can lead to misleading, biased, and incomplete insight during the interviews and surveys. Also, the students who choose to participate in design research might not be representative of the broader student population, particularly those with the most severe needs or those who are most withdrawn. This leads to a biased understanding of the core problems
Another limitation is the difficulty of measuring if the app, designed through the design thinking process, is actually improving mental health outcomes (e.g., reducing anxiety, depression, or drop-out rates) versus simply improving user engagement. Because Design Thinking relies on fast, repeated testing, it does not allow enough time to measure how well the mental wellness app works over many months or years. To truly understand its long-term impact, a more extended evaluation would be necessary.
Additionally, Mental health needs are highly personal. A solution that works for a small group of tested students might not scale effectively or adapt to the diverse cultural, economic, and academic pressures faced by the entire university population.
The project will also face resource constraints in terms of development time and personnel. Given that the app will be developed by a limited team or individual, access to advanced tools, professional therapists, or large user groups may be limited. This may result in the reduction of the effectiveness of certain Design Thinking stages, especially testing and evaluation.
Ultimately, the Design Thinking approach generates solutions based on user experience, not medical standards. This may result in a final product that is user-friendly, may still require professional validation from mental health experts to ensure safety and appropriateness.

CHAPTER TWO 
LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Introduction
Mental health refers to the way an individual thinks, feels, and behaves, influencing how they cope with stress, relate to others, and make decisions. It plays a vital role in shaping one’s overall well-being and ability to function effectively in daily life (Felman & Tee-Melegrito, 2024). For students, mental health is essential, as it affects their concentration, motivation, academic performance, and social relationships. However, many students experience various mental health challenges such as anxiety, stress, depression, and burnout due to academic pressure, financial struggles, social isolation, and the transition to independent living. When not properly managed, these issues can interfere with learning, reduce productivity, and negatively impact both emotional stability and academic success.
Mental health has become a crucial concern among university students due to the combined pressures of academic performance, financial stress, poor living conditions, transportation challenges, digital and technological pressures, social adjustment, and personal development. These challenges often contribute to heightened levels of anxiety, depression, and emotional distress within campus communities. With the rise of digital technology, mobile applications have become increasingly popular to provide accessible and scalable mental wellness support. However, despite the proliferation of such applications, many fail to fully engage users or address their genuine needs due to the limited use of user-centered design strategies.
The Design Thinking approach, which emphasizes empathy, definition, ideation, prototyping, and testing, offers a promising framework for creating digital solutions that are both functional and emotionally resonant. Applying the design thinking approach to designing and building a mental wellness app for university students ensures that their unique needs and contexts are applied during the design process.

2.2 Understanding Mental Health
Mental health refers to a person’s emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It affects how individuals think, feel, and act, as well as how they manage stress, interact with others, and make everyday decisions. According to the World Health Organization (WHO, 2025), “Mental health is a state of mental well-being that enables people to cope with the stresses of life, realize their abilities, learn and work well, and contribute to their community. It has intrinsic and instrumental value and is a basic human right.” Furthermore, the World Health Organization reports that one in four people will experience a mental health condition at some point in their lives, highlighting that these conditions can affect anyone regardless of age, gender, or background. Such experiences can have a significant impact on personal relationships, work performance, and overall quality of life. Therefore, mental health is crucial to personal, community, and socio-economic development.
Moreover, mental health is more than the absence of mental disorders, as it can be seen as a broad spectrum that people experience in different ways. At any point in time, a mix of personal, family, social, and environmental factors can either promote or harm an individual’s mental well-being. While many people can recover from difficulties, those who face challenging or negative life situations are more likely to develop mental health problems. These problems may involve mental disorders, emotional or social disabilities, and other conditions that cause distress, reduce daily functioning, or lead to self-harming behavior. Although effective and affordable treatments exist, most health systems across the world still lack enough resources, leaving many people without proper mental health care.
In addition, just like physical health, mental health is not a permanent state. It exists on a continuum that ranges from optimal well-being and positive functioning to severe mental health challenges. Throughout a person’s life, their mental health can shift along this spectrum depending on various life experiences, stressors, and personal circumstances. Recognizing this dynamic nature of mental health is essential, as it emphasizes the need for continuous support, awareness, and interventions that promote emotional resilience and overall well-being.
2.3 Factors that Increase the Risk of Mental Health Issues
Mental health is influenced by many different factors that can either protect or harm a person’s well-being. These factors exist at the individual, family, community, and societal levels. When they are positive, they help people stay emotionally strong and stable, but when they are negative, they can increase the chances of developing mental health problems.
Research indicates that mental health issues are becoming more prevalent among children and young people. Studies estimate that about 13% of individuals aged 5 to 24 experience some form of mental disorder. The World Health Organization (WHO, 2025) also reports that half of all mental health conditions start before age 14, and nearly three-quarters develop by the mid-20s. This suggests that many mental health problems begin early in life, yet many young people do not receive adequate care. Reports reveal that between 64% and 87% of these conditions go undetected or untreated (Scholten & Granic, 2019).
Risk factors can appear at different stages of life, but those that occur during sensitive developmental periods, especially in early childhood and adolescence, are particularly harmful. For example, harsh parenting, bullying, or physical punishment can affect a child’s emotional development and increase the likelihood of mental health conditions later in life.
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), several factors at both the individual and social levels can increase the risk of mental health problems. Personal traits such as genetic vulnerability, poor emotional regulation, and substance use can heighten the likelihood of developing mental health conditions, while strong coping skills, emotional intelligence, and healthy habits can act as protective factors. Moreover, broader social and environmental conditions—including poverty, violence, discrimination, unemployment, and inadequate housing—can negatively affect mental well-being. In contrast, supportive communities that provide access to quality education, employment, and safe living environments tend to promote better mental health outcomes.
In addition to personal and social influences, global factors such as economic hardship, disease outbreaks, displacement, and climate change also affect people’s mental well-being. These large-scale challenges often create uncertainty, stress, and emotional exhaustion that can impact individuals and communities around the world.
Poor mental health among university students has become a growing public health concern. Studies show a steady increase in mental health problems such as depression and anxiety, with recent surveys reporting that one in five students has a current diagnosis, and almost half have experienced serious psychological distress. Factors contributing to this rise include academic pressure, financial stress from increased tuition fees and student loans, together with the challenges of transitioning to independent living. The COVID-19 pandemic further worsened the situation, leading to higher rates of anxiety, depression, and eating disorders among young adults. Despite the growing demand for support, many students do not seek help due to stigma or lack of awareness, highlighting the need for universities to adopt more accessible and innovative mental health interventions (Campbell et al., 2022; Pedrelli et al., 2022).
Overall, these risk factors affect people in different ways depending on their situations and environments. However, university students are especially exposed to several of these stressors due to academic workload, financial pressure, and social transitions. The next section discusses the specific mental health challenges faced by university students.

2.4 Mental Health Challenges among University Students
University students are a group that is especially vulnerable to mental health issues. These stresses and problems can harm their health and academic success (Wyatt & Oswalt, 2013). Moving to college often involves more academic pressure, financial worries, social changes, and the need for independence. These stressors can lead to anxiety, depression, and other mental health problems if not managed properly.
Attending college can be a particularly demanding time for students, as they balance academic pressures with the challenges of gaining independence from their families. Many students also juggle work or family responsibilities, which can raise stress levels. This transitional period may trigger the onset of mental health or substance use issues or worsen existing conditions. Since college students face unique developmental and environmental factors, it is important to consider specific aspects when addressing their mental health. Effective interventions often involve engaging parents, coordinating with other service providers, and utilizing technology to enhance treatment adherence. Awareness of these distinct challenges enables practitioners to provide more targeted support for this population (Pedrelli, Nyer, Yeung, Zulauf, & Wilens, 2015).
A recent longitudinal study by Porru, Schuring, Bültmann, Portoghese, Burdorf, and Robroek (2022) supports these observations, showing that university students face a variety of challenges that can affect their mental health, some of which are directly related to their academic environment, while others are unrelated to the university itself. Non-university-related challenges include financial pressure and limited social support, which have been linked to poorer mental health outcomes. University-related challenges include excessive academic workload, inadequate feedback, strained relationships with staff, a lack of peer support, concerns about future professional competence, and limited interest in coursework. Both types of challenges can contribute to increased stress and psychological difficulties, highlighting the need to consider the diverse sources of pressure students experience when addressing their mental well-being.
In addition, the widespread use of smartphones and social media has significantly changed how college students interact and connect. Still, it has also contributed to the rise of mental health issues, including depression and anxiety (Cain et al., 2020). Research suggests that excessive or passive engagement with social media—such as continuously comparing oneself to others or “lurking” without active interaction—can exacerbate feelings of isolation, stress, and low self-esteem. While these technologies offer opportunities for social connection, academic engagement, and access to digital mental health interventions, misuse or overuse appears to correlate with increased psychological distress among students. The ubiquity of personal computing technology has created a new environment in which mental health challenges are amplified, highlighting the need for moderation, guided use, and the development of technology-enabled interventions that support, rather than hinder, student well-being (Lattie, Lipson, & Eisenberg, 2019).
In summary, university students face numerous challenges that can significantly affect their mental health and overall well-being. The transition to higher education introduces academic, financial, and social pressures that often heighten stress and emotional difficulties. These challenges, combined with the growing influence of digital technology and social media, make this population particularly vulnerable to issues such as anxiety and depression. Understanding these factors is essential for developing effective mental health interventions that address students’ unique experiences and promote a healthier, more supportive learning environment.


2.5 Mental Health Challenges Among University Students in Nigeria
Mental health challenges are increasingly prevalent among university students in Nigeria, who make up a significant portion of the adolescent population and are undergoing a critical transition from dependence to independence, marked by rapid physical, mental, and social changes (Kukoyi et al., 2022). 
According to the World Health Organization (2017), the majority of mental health problems occur between the ages of 15 and 24 years, underscoring that mental well-being is a vital component of overall health. Studies have shown that tertiary institution students are particularly vulnerable to various health problems, including psychological stress, emotional disorders, and unhealthy lifestyles (Oswalt et al., 2020; Basta et al., 2022).
Mental disorders account for about 11.5% of the global disease burden (WHO, 2007), and approximately 20% of Nigerians are reported to suffer from some form of mental illness (Shekihar, 2006). Despite this, mental health remains one of the most neglected areas in Nigeria’s public health system. Although a Mental Health Policy was formulated in 1991, its poor implementation has resulted in limited support services, stigmatization, and societal neglect of affected individuals (Mental Health Leadership and Advocacy Program, 2012). Consequently, mental health issues have been found to negatively affect students’ academic performance, leading to reduced concentration, absenteeism, and poor academic outcomes (Brackney & Karabenick, 1995). The absence of early identification and intervention systems in Nigerian universities further worsens the situation, highlighting the urgent need for institutional and policy-based responses to support students’ mental well-being.
Nigeria’s educational system is also known to be highly rigorous. As one of the most populous countries in the world, with over 222 million people—about 70% of whom are under 30—the pressure on higher education is immense. In 2024, there were approximately 1.8 million undergraduate students and 242,000 postgraduate students in Nigerian universities, with over 1.2 million enrolled in federal universities and about 103,000 in private universities. Federal universities, in particular, face persistent and severe challenges such as inadequate funding, recurring Academic Staff Union of Universities (ASUU) strikes, poor and decaying infrastructure, insecurity, and inefficient administrative systems. These challenges collectively contribute to heightened stress levels and declining mental well-being among students.
Despite the increasing awareness of mental health challenges among university students in Nigeria, access to effective mental health care remains limited due to inadequate infrastructure, a shortage of professionals, and persistent social stigma surrounding mental illness. Traditional counseling services in most universities are often underfunded or inaccessible, leaving many students without timely support. In recent years, however, digital technology has emerged as a promising solution to bridge this gap. Mobile applications, online therapy platforms, and artificial intelligence (AI)-based tools now provide innovative ways to promote mental wellness, offering students convenient and confidential access to psychological support. This shift toward digital mental health care forms the focus of the next section, after the effects and causes of mental health challenges.
In summary, mental health challenges among university students in Nigeria represent a growing public health concern that is deeply intertwined with social, academic, and institutional factors. Despite the existence of policies and increasing awareness, the lack of effective implementation, inadequate mental health infrastructure, and persistent stigma continue to hinder progress. The combination of academic pressure, poor learning environments, and limited access to professional support has placed students at greater risk of psychological distress. However, the emergence of digital mental health innovations offers a new and promising pathway to addressing these challenges. As universities and policymakers explore sustainable solutions, integrating technology-driven interventions could play a crucial role in improving students’ mental well-being and fostering healthier learning environments.

2.6 Causes and Effects of Mental Health Issues Among University Students
Mental health issues among university students arise from a complex interaction of personal, academic, social, and environmental factors. These causes often overlap, creating a cycle of stress and emotional strain that affects students’ well-being and academic performance.
2.6.1 Causes of Mental Health Issues
University students in Nigeria face a range of factors that can negatively impact their mental health. Academic pressure is a major contributor, as students are expected to handle heavy workloads, frequent examinations, and tight deadlines, often with inadequate support. The frequent strikes by the Academic Staff Union of Universities (ASUU) disrupt academic schedules, creating uncertainty and stress. Financial challenges are another significant factor, as many students struggle to pay tuition, accommodation, and living expenses, sometimes relying on part-time jobs that add to their stress. Social factors, such as limited peer support, family expectations, and difficulties adapting to the university environment, also affect students’ emotional well-being. Additionally, poor infrastructure, overcrowded lecture halls, and limited access to counseling services exacerbate stress and anxiety, while exposure to insecurity, campus violence, and societal pressures further increase vulnerability to mental health problems.
2.6.2 Effects of Mental Health Issues
The consequences of poor mental health among university students are far-reaching. Students experiencing psychological stress, anxiety, or depression often show decreased concentration, memory difficulties, and lower academic performance. This can lead to absenteeism, delayed graduation, or even dropout. Emotional and behavioral effects include irritability, social withdrawal, an;d reduced motivation, which can harm relationships with peers, faculty, and family. Mental health challenges can also contribute to unhealthy coping mechanisms, such as substance abuse or self-harm. Over time, these issues can reduce overall quality of life, hinder personal development, and negatively impact students’ readiness to transition successfully into professional and adult life.

2.7 Digital Mental Wellness Interventions
Digital wellness refers to the impact of digital technology on our mental, physical, social, emotional, and spiritual well-being. Within this broader concept, digital mental wellness interventions are technology-based tools and applications designed to promote emotional well-being, reduce stress, and provide psychological support. These interventions have become increasingly popular among university students due to their accessibility, affordability, and convenience. By integrating mental health care into everyday technology use, they help bridge the gap between individuals and professional services, especially in environments where mental health resources are limited.
There are various categories of digital mental wellness tools, each addressing different aspects of mental health. Mood-tracking apps such as Daylio and Moodpath enable users to monitor their emotions and identify behavioral patterns that may signal psychological changes. Similarly, mindfulness and meditation platforms like Headspace and Calm teach relaxation techniques and promote self-awareness, helping to decrease anxiety and improve concentration, particularly among students. Furthermore, AI-powered chatbots such as Wysa and Woebot use cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) principles to provide conversational support, helping users challenge negative thoughts and develop healthier coping strategies. These tools are especially valuable for young adults who may hesitate to seek in-person therapy due to stigma or limited access to counseling.
Research evidence underscores the growing effectiveness and relevance of these digital tools. Firth et al. (2017) found that mobile-based interventions significantly reduced symptoms of depression and anxiety across diverse populations. Scholten and Granic (2019) further observed that integrating design thinking principles enhances user engagement and satisfaction with digital interventions. More recently, Löchner et al. (2025) reviewed advancements in e-health technologies, emphasizing the importance of ethical standards, data privacy, and trust in ensuring sustainable use. Collectively, these findings highlight that well-designed digital tools not only expand access to care but also have the potential to improve mental health outcomes among students.
Beyond accessibility and efficacy, digital mental wellness interventions also address key barriers faced by students. As mHealth (mobile health) tools, they provide flexible and continuous support that complements traditional therapy. Students can access guidance at any time and from anywhere, overcoming the geographical and scheduling limitations of in-person counseling. Additionally, their anonymous nature reduces stigma and encourages help-seeking behavior (Wiljer et al., 2020). Such convenience is especially critical in university settings, where many students experience stress yet have limited time or resources to seek professional help.
Several studies further demonstrate their practical benefits. Biofeedback-based and mindfulness apps, for example, have been shown to improve emotional regulation and enhance self-awareness (Ponzo et al., 2020). Likewise, mobile applications have proven acceptable, engaging, and cost-effective for large student populations, offering scalable solutions without requiring extensive infrastructure (Oliveira et al., 2021). Together, these findings illustrate the vital role of digital mental wellness tools in promoting emotional resilience and psychological well-being among university students.
Ultimately, the growing adoption of digital mental wellness interventions reflects a significant shift in how mental health support is delivered. As technology continues to influence students’ daily lives, these tools provide an innovative means of fostering well-being in accessible, personalized, and stigma-free ways. When thoughtfully designed and ethically implemented, they can complement traditional mental health services and empower students to take an active role in maintaining their emotional health.

2.7.1 Limitations of Digital Mental Wellness Interventions
Despite the growing promise of digital mental wellness interventions, several limitations continue to affect their effectiveness and long-term impact. One of the major challenges lies in user engagement and adherence, as many individuals lose motivation after initial use. Studies have shown that a significant number of participants drop out before completing most of the intervention modules, particularly in unguided programs that lack therapist support. This disengagement reduces the overall effectiveness of these interventions and limits their ability to produce lasting behavioral change (Scholten & Granic, 2019).
In addition to low engagement, cultural fit and inclusivity present another critical limitation. Many digital mental health tools are not designed to reflect the values, beliefs, and lived experiences of diverse populations. For example, Johanna et al. (2025) emphasized the importance of developing e-mental health screening tools that are culturally safe for Indigenous youth. Their findings indicate that interventions failing to integrate cultural perspectives are often perceived as impersonal or irrelevant, which reduces trust and participation. This highlights the need for digital solutions that acknowledge cultural diversity and promote inclusiveness in both design and implementation.
Moreover, the issue of limited personalization further constrains their effectiveness. Many existing digital interventions adopt a one-size-fits-all approach, offering minimal flexibility to accommodate individual preferences or needs. In most cases, these programs replicate traditional therapy manuals in digital form, making them less engaging for young users who are accustomed to interactive and dynamic online environments (Scholten & Granic, 2019).
Taken together, these challenges suggest that to maximize their potential, future digital mental wellness interventions must go beyond basic functionality. By enhancing user engagement, ensuring cultural relevance, and integrating adaptive, personalized features, developers can create tools that are not only more effective but also more meaningful and sustainable for diverse student populations.

2.8 Overview of the Design Thinking Approach
Design Thinking is a problem-solving approach centered on understanding the needs and experiences of users. It integrates empathy, creativity, and analytical thinking to develop innovative solutions that genuinely meet user expectations. Many modern organizations adopt Design Thinking to strengthen customer engagement, enhance user experiences, and improve product effectiveness.
It follows a flexible, iterative, and non-linear process that helps teams explore complex or poorly defined problems. The process involves five key stages — Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test — which guide designers in understanding users, redefining challenges, generating ideas, and developing and refining effective solutions. 


2.8.1 Stages of Design Thinking
The Design Thinking process consists of five iterative stages that guide teams toward user-centered innovation. The first stage, Empathize, focuses on understanding users’ experiences and challenges through research, observation, and engagement. This helps designers gain authentic insights into users’ needs rather than relying on assumptions. The second stage, Define, involves analyzing and synthesizing the gathered information to identify core challenges and frame them as clear, human-centered problem statements. In the Ideate stage, teams generate a wide range of creative solutions, thinking beyond conventional approaches to challenge existing assumptions and develop innovative ideas. The fourth stage, Prototype, emphasizes experimentation, where designers create low-cost, simplified models or mockups to visualize and explore possible solutions. Finally, the Test stage involves evaluating these prototypes with real users to collect feedback, refine the solutions, and determine whether they effectively address user needs. Importantly, these stages are not strictly linear; they are iterative modes that may overlap or repeat, allowing teams to continuously improve their understanding and design outcomes.

2.8.2 Importance of Design Thinking in App Design
Design thinking is an essential approach that fosters creativity, innovation, and problem-solving. It engages individuals in an iterative process of experimentation, prototyping, and feedback gathering, which helps refine ideas and develop effective solutions. By combining analytical and creative thinking, design thinking bridges the gap between theory and practice, allowing learners and designers to apply conceptual knowledge in real-world contexts. Furthermore, it emphasizes user-centered understanding, enabling individuals to design solutions that truly address user needs. Overall, design thinking promotes continuous learning, adaptability, and the development of critical problem-solving skills that are vital in the 21st century (Razzouk & Shute, 2012).
In the context of app design, Design Thinking is particularly relevant because it places users at the center of the development process. By emphasizing empathy and understanding users’ pain points, designers can create applications that are more intuitive, accessible, and aligned with users’ real-life experiences. This human-centered focus ensures that the final product not only functions effectively but also provides a meaningful and satisfying user experience.
Moreover, the iterative nature of Design Thinking encourages continuous testing and refinement. Through prototyping and user feedback, developers can identify flaws early, reduce costly errors, and enhance the app’s usability and performance. This process supports creativity, innovation, and flexibility, allowing design teams to build solutions that genuinely solve users’ problems and adapt to their evolving needs.
In summary, Design Thinking serves as a powerful framework for developing digital products that are both functional and user-centered. Its emphasis on empathy, iteration, and problem-solving enables designers to move beyond technical efficiency toward creating experiences that truly resonate with users. By integrating Design Thinking into app development, teams can ensure that their solutions are not only innovative but also meaningful, sustainable, and impactful in addressing real-world challenges.

2.9 Application of Design Thinking in Mental Wellness App Development
Design Thinking offers a structured, user-centered approach to developing mental wellness applications that effectively address users’ emotional and psychological needs. By emphasizing empathy, creativity, and iterative testing, this process ensures that digital mental health interventions are not only functional but also engaging, accessible, and relevant to users’ real-life experiences. The five stages of Design Thinking—Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test,  can be strategically applied to create mental wellness apps that prioritize user satisfaction and therapeutic impact.
In the Empathize stage, developers and researchers seek to understand users’ emotional challenges, behaviors, and coping mechanisms through interviews, surveys, and observations. This helps uncover underlying issues such as stress, anxiety, or social isolation that students may experience. The Define stage involves synthesizing these insights into clear problem statements, such as identifying the need for more accessible, stigma-free mental health support for university students. During the Ideate stage, teams brainstorm innovative ideas, including features like chatbots for emotional support, mood trackers, or mindfulness reminders, to address the identified needs. The Prototype stage translates these ideas into low-cost app mockups or interactive models that visualize the proposed solutions and allow for early feedback. Finally, in the Test stage, these prototypes are evaluated with real users to determine usability, effectiveness, and engagement levels. The insights gained often lead to refinements or a return to earlier stages, ensuring the final app aligns closely with user expectations and therapeutic goals.
This iterative application of Design Thinking promotes continuous improvement and ensures that mental wellness apps are designed around user’s lived experiences rather than theoretical assumptions. As a result, the approach enhances engagement, reduces barriers to help-seeking, and supports the creation of evidence-based digital tools that can improve mental well-being among students and other populations.

2.10 Review of Existing Studies 
Design Thinking has proven valuable in developing digital mental health interventions that are user-centered, engaging, and effective. Scholten and Granic (2019) applied Design Thinking principles to create digital tools for youth mental health, emphasizing empathy, multidisciplinary ideation, and experimentation. They co-designed interactive games such as MindLight an anxiety-prevention game, and a smoking cessation game with young users to ensure relevance and engagement. Through empathy-driven participatory design, they involved youth directly in the development process, addressing the motivational and emotional needs often overlooked in traditional interventions. Iterative prototyping and testing led to significant reductions in children’s anxiety symptoms across multiple randomized controlled trials, demonstrating the potential of Design Thinking to produce both effective and enjoyable mental wellness tools.
Similarly, Sam, Richardson, and Currie (2022) combined Design Thinking with Indigenous storytelling through a Two-Eyed Seeing approach to co-create an e-Mental health screening app for Indigenous youth. Participants engaged in Design Circles to build persona stories, map experiences, and provide usability feedback. This iterative and collaborative process incorporated cultural knowledge, holistic wellness concepts, and user preferences, ensuring that the final tools were culturally safe, relevant, and responsive to the needs of Indigenous youth.
Expanding on these applications, Bernaerts et al. (2025) used the Design Thinking approach in the SUPER project to adapt two digital mental health apps SAM and SAFE for use in multiple countries. The process followed the five iterative stages of Design Thinking: empathize (gathering insights from users, clinicians, and stakeholders through interviews and focus groups); define (identifying user needs, challenges, and goals using personas and journey maps); ideate (generating creative solutions to address the defined challenges); prototype (developing translated and interactive versions of the apps); and test (collecting user and clinician feedback for refinement). This comprehensive and participatory process emphasized cultural adaptation, stakeholder involvement, and iterative development, ensuring that the apps were contextually relevant and user-centered.
Overall, these studies highlight the versatility and effectiveness of Design Thinking in digital mental health innovation. By combining empathy, cultural inclusivity, and iterative design, this approach fosters the creation of mental health tools that are not only evidence-based but also engaging, culturally sensitive, and tailored to users’ real-world needs.

2.11 Gaps in the Literature
Although existing studies demonstrate that Design Thinking can support the creation of engaging digital mental health tools, certain gaps remain. Scholten and Granic (2019) focused primarily on youth but did not involve students as active co-designers, and their findings may not be applicable across different cultural or regional contexts. Similarly, the study by Sam et al. (2022), which integrated Design Thinking with Indigenous storytelling to ensure cultural relevance, was limited to Indigenous youth and did not examine the long-term use or effectiveness of the intervention. In the SUPER project, Bernaerts et al. (2025) adapted the SAM and SAFE apps for use in multiple countries, yet the research mainly involved adult stakeholders, with minimal participation from student users. Furthermore, the long-term impact and real-world adoption of these apps were not evaluated. Taken together, these gaps highlight the need for studies that apply Design Thinking in African contexts, actively involve students as co-creators, and assess the sustained effectiveness and continued use of digital mental health apps.

This study seeks to address these gaps by applying the Design Thinking approach to the development of a digital mental wellness app tailored for university students in Nigeria. By engaging students directly in the design process, the study aims to create an app that is both user-centered and culturally relevant, contributing to improved mental well-being and expanding the understanding of Design Thinking in digital health innovation.

3.4 Design Thinking Framework
The Design Thinking framework was employed in this study to guide the systematic development of the mental wellness application. This framework consists of five interrelated stages: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test. Each stage was applied to ensure that the application was developed based on a clear understanding of users’ mental wellness needs and refined through continuous user involvement. The stages are described as follows.
Empathize Stage: The empathize stage focused on understanding the mental wellness challenges experienced by undergraduate students. At this stage, the study sought to gain insight into students’ emotional states, stressors, academic pressures, and coping mechanisms. Data were collected through user-centered methods such as questionnaires and informal discussions, which allowed participants to express their experiences and concerns related to mental health. This stage enabled the researcher to develop empathy for the users and to understand the problem from the users’ perspectives rather than making assumptions.
Define Stage: In the define stage, the information gathered during the empathize phase was analyzed and synthesized to clearly identify the core mental wellness problems faced by students. This stage helped to narrow the focus of the study by transforming broad mental health concerns into specific, actionable problems that the application aimed to address.
Ideate Stage: The ideate stage involved generating possible solutions and features that could address the identified mental wellness challenges. Brainstorming techniques were used to explore a wide range of ideas without immediate evaluation or limitation. At this stage, potential application features such as stress management tools, mood tracking, motivational content, and mental wellness resources were considered. The goal of this stage was to explore creative and user-focused solutions that aligned with the defined problem statement.
Prototype Stage: During the prototype stage, selected ideas from the ideation phase were transformed into a visual and interactive representation of the application. This stage allowed the researcher to translate abstract ideas into tangible designs that could be evaluated by users.
Test Stage: The test stage involved evaluating the developed prototype with potential users to assess its usability, clarity, and relevance to their mental wellness needs. Users were asked to interact with the prototype and provide feedback on the design, navigation, and overall user experience. The feedback collected at this stage was used to identify areas for improvement and to refine the application design. This stage ensured that the final design decisions were informed by user input and aligned with the objectives of the study.

The frontend is responsible for delivering an engaging user interface, facilitating smooth user interaction, and enabling intuitive navigation throughout the app. Design efforts will focus on usability, accessibility, and consistency to ensure that users can easily record moods, view patterns, and interact with app features across different devices.
React Native: React Native will be used to build the frontend, allowing the development of cross-platform mobile apps with a single JavaScript codebase. It is based on ReactJS, an open-source framework, ensuring efficient development and maintainability.
Expo Router: Expo Router is a file-based router for React Native and web applications, enabling seamless navigation between screens. It allows components to be reused across platforms, enhancing the consistency and usability of the app interface.
These technologies will enhance development speed and ensure a smooth, responsive interface across Android and iOS platforms.
3.8.3 Back-end Development
The backend of the Wellnest mental wellness mobile application is responsible for handling authentication, processing mood entries, generating AI-based recommendations, and managing application data. It ensures secure data handling through proper data protection measures and role-based access, safeguarding user information while supporting efficient operations.
Node.js: Node.js is an open-source, cross-platform JavaScript runtime environment that executes JavaScript code outside a web browser. It enables developers to build server-side applications efficiently using JavaScript.
Express.js: Express.js is a minimal and flexible Node.js web application framework that simplifies routing, middleware integration, and server configuration. It facilitates smooth data transactions and server-side operations for both web and mobile applications.
3.8.4 Development Tools
The development of the Wellnest mental wellness mobile application involved the use of several tools that facilitated efficient coding, testing, debugging, and design. Figma:

Git & GitHub:

CHAPTER FIVE
SUMMARY, CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATION
5.1 Summary
The design and implementation of the WellNest Mental Wellness Application were motivated by the growing need to provide university students with accessible, convenient, and user-centered mental health support. Traditional mental health services are often limited by barriers such as stigma, scheduling constraints, and limited access to professional support. These challenges highlighted the need for a digital solution that promotes self-care while providing access to professional counseling.
To address these challenges, the WellNest Mental Wellness Application was developed as a mobile-based platform that supports students in monitoring and improving their mental well-being. The application provides a range of features, including mood tracking, journaling, guided meditation sessions, an AI chatbot, mental wellness resources, motivational quotes, and appointment booking with licensed psychologists. In addition, psychologists can manage their availability and appointments, while administrators oversee users, psychologists, resources, and overall platform operations.
The application was implemented using modern software development technologies, with React Native for the mobile application, Node.js and Express.js for the backend services, and MongoDB for database management. The client-server architecture facilitates efficient communication between students, psychologists, and administrators while ensuring reliable data management and system performance.
Through the application of user-centered design principles and the implementation of essential mental wellness features, the WellNest Mental Wellness Application successfully achieved its intended objectives by providing an accessible, functional, and engaging platform that supports the mental well-being of university students.
5.2 Conclusion
The WellNest Mental Wellness Application provides an effective digital solution for supporting the mental well-being of university students. By integrating self-care tools with access to professional psychological services, the application enables students to actively monitor their emotional health and seek timely support whenever necessary.
The implementation of features such as mood tracking, journaling, guided meditation, AI-powered assistance, educational resources, motivational content, and online appointment scheduling contributes to a comprehensive mental wellness experience. The dedicated interfaces for psychologists and administrators further ensure efficient management of counseling services and platform content.
The results obtained from system testing and user acceptance testing demonstrate that the application is functional, reliable, user-friendly, and capable of meeting its intended objectives. Overall, the WellNest Mental Wellness Application represents a practical and scalable approach to promoting mental wellness within university communities and has the potential to improve access to mental health support through technology.
5.3 Recommendations
To enhance the effectiveness and long-term sustainability of the WellNest Mental Wellness Application, the following recommendations are proposed:
The application should be deployed on a secure and scalable cloud infrastructure to improve reliability, availability, and data security.
Push notification services should be enhanced to provide timely reminders for mood tracking, meditation sessions, appointments, and other wellness activities.
Additional AI capabilities should be incorporated to deliver more personalized wellness recommendations and conversational support based on users' interaction history.
More guided meditation sessions, wellness exercises, and educational resources should be added regularly to maintain user engagement and address diverse mental wellness needs.
A secure video consultation feature should be integrated to enable psychologists to conduct virtual counseling sessions directly within the application.
Regular maintenance, security updates, and performance optimization should be carried out to ensure the application remains reliable and protected against emerging security threats.
A feedback and rating system should be incorporated to allow students to evaluate their counseling sessions and provide suggestions for improving the platform.
Future versions of the application could integrate wearable health devices and mood analytics to provide deeper insights into users' emotional well-being and support more personalized mental health interventions.
