1.7 Operational Definition of Key Terms
   E-learning: The use of computers and other digital technologies to deliver educational content and support learning outside a traditional classroom. It allows learners to access materials and participate in learning activities anytime and from any location through online platforms.
Accessibility: The design of digital systems in a way that enables people with disabilities to perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with content effectively. It ensures that users with disabilities can use digital platforms independently and without unnecessary difficulty.
Assistive Technology: Devices or software that help individuals with disabilities access and use digital information. Examples include screen readers, screen magnifiers, speech-to-text tools, and alternative input devices.
Universal Design for Learning (UDL): A framework for designing learning environments that support all learners by providing multiple ways to access information, stay engaged, and demonstrate learning. UDL recognizes that learners differ in how they learn and promotes flexible learning experiences that accommodate these differences.
Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG): International standards developed to guide the creation of accessible web content. WCAG provides principles and success criteria to help ensure that websites and web applications can be used by people with a wide range of disabilities.















CHAPTER TWO
LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Introduction
Literature review is important in a design and implementation study because it provides the concepts, theories, and technical benchmarks needed to build a credible system. In this study, it explains e-learning and digital accessibility, highlights key accessibility standards, and reviews how existing e-learning platforms support (or fail to support) students with disabilities, so the proposed system is informed by prior work (Seale, 2014).
Although e-learning and LMS platforms have expanded access and flexibility, their benefits are not evenly shared. When accessibility is ignored, digital learning can exclude students with visual, hearing, motor, and cognitive impairments (Seale, 2014; Selwyn, 2016). Accessibility is therefore both a technical and equity issue—barriers like missing captions, poor screen-reader labels, or lack of keyboard navigation reduce participation and can worsen educational inequality (W3C, 2018; UNESCO, 2020).
2.2 Concept of E-Learning
E-learning (electronic learning) refers to the use of digital technologies to deliver learning content, facilitate instruction, and support interaction among learners and instructors. E-learning typically relies on internet technologies to distribute content and enable communication, thereby improving knowledge acquisition and performance (Rosenberg, 2001). In higher education, e-learning has become a mainstream mode of instruction, supporting fully online, blended, and hybrid learning arrangements.
Beyond content delivery, contemporary e-learning environments are expected to support the full learning cycle: access to course materials, learner–instructor communication, peer collaboration, assessment, feedback, and academic record management. Many institutions implement these functions through Learning Management Systems (LMS) such as Moodle, Blackboard, and Canvas. These systems provide structured course spaces where lecturers can post resources, set assignments, grade submissions, and communicate announcements, while students can engage in discussions, submit work, and track progress (Anderson, 2008).
2.2.1 Evolution of E-Learning
The evolution of e-learning can be described in phases. Early e-learning models largely focused on digitizing existing classroom materials—converting lecture notes into PDF documents or basic web pages and distributing them through institutional portals. Later developments emphasized interactivity by introducing multimedia learning resources, discussion forums, quizzes, and synchronous tools such as live video sessions. With improvements in bandwidth and device availability, e-learning expanded to include learning analytics, mobile access, and personalized learning supports (Ally, 2019; Holmes et al., 2022).
More recently, emerging trends such as adaptive learning systems and AI-supported tutoring have gained attention. These approaches use data about learner interaction to recommend resources, flag learning difficulties, and support personalized learning paths (Holmes et al., 2022). While such innovations can enhance learning outcomes, they also introduce new accessibility concerns, especially when personalization features are not designed to work with assistive technologies or when interfaces become overly complex for some learners.
2.2.2 Forms and Models of E-Learning
E-learning can be implemented through several forms. Fully online learning occurs when teaching, learning activities, and assessments are conducted entirely through digital platforms. Blended learning combines face-to-face instruction with online components, while hybrid learning usually describes flexible arrangements in which some learners attend physically and others participate remotely. These approaches differ in delivery style but share common reliance on digital platforms for content management, communication, and assessment (Garrison & Anderson, 2003).
From a pedagogical perspective, e-learning environments may also be explained using interaction models. Moore (1989) identifies learner–content, learner–instructor, and learner–learner interaction as essential dimensions of distance education. Effective platforms support all three interactions by enabling access to content, providing communication channels for feedback, and supporting collaborative learning activities such as forums, group work, and peer review.
2.2.3 Key Features of an LMS
An LMS typically includes content management tools (uploading and organizing learning materials), assessment tools (quizzes, assignments, grading), communication tools (forums, messaging, announcements), and tracking tools (attendance, activity logs, progress monitoring). In addition, modern LMS platforms often integrate external tools such as video conferencing, cloud storage, and plagiarism detection services. These features make LMS platforms attractive for institutions, but the complexity of these systems can also present usability and accessibility challenges if inclusive design principles are not applied.
Multimedia learning is a major strength of e-learning platforms, as learning resources can be provided in text, audio, video, and interactive formats. However, research on multimedia learning emphasizes that design quality matters: poorly designed multimedia can increase cognitive load and reduce learning effectiveness, particularly for learners who require accessible formats (Mayer, 2009; Sweller, 2019).
Therefore, accessible design must include not only technical compatibility but also clarity of presentation and reduction of unnecessary complexity.
2.2.4 Benefits and Challenges of E-Learning
E-learning offers widely recognized benefits, including flexibility of time and location, scalability of instruction, efficient distribution of resources, and support for self-paced learning. Students can revisit materials, access learning resources repeatedly, and manage learning schedules around other commitments. Institutionally, e-learning can support large class sizes, reduce printing costs, and provide consistent access to course information (Ally, 2019).
Despite these benefits, scholars warn that e-learning can reinforce educational inequality where access to devices, stable internet, and inclusive interfaces is limited. Challenges include reduced learner motivation in poorly designed online environments, limited digital literacy for some users, and technical barriers such as unreliable connectivity (Selwyn, 2016). For students with disabilities, these barriers are compounded when platforms are not designed for screen-reader use, lack captions, or rely on interaction patterns that exclude keyboard-only users.
Consequently, e-learning holds both promise and risk for students with disabilities. On one hand, digital platforms can provide alternative content formats, flexible assessment arrangements, and access to assistive technologies. On the other hand, inaccessible interfaces and poorly structured digital content can restrict participation. This dual nature reinforces the need for accessibility to be treated as a central requirement in e-learning platform design.
2.3 Digital Accessibility in Education
Digital accessibility refers to the design of digital content and systems in ways that enable persons with disabilities to perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with technology effectively. In education, accessibility ensures that learning materials, assessments, communication tools, and platform interfaces are usable by all students regardless of sensory, physical, or cognitive limitations (W3C, 2018). Accessible e-learning is therefore a practical expression of inclusive education in a digital environment.
2.3.1 Accessibility and Inclusive Education
Inclusive education is grounded in the principle that all learners should have equal opportunities to participate and succeed in learning. Within digital learning contexts, this implies that platforms should be designed to support diverse learners from the outset rather than requiring learners to request accommodations after barriers are encountered. International policy discussions increasingly emphasize inclusive digital learning as part of equitable education systems (UNESCO, 2020).
Accessibility also supports independence and privacy for students with disabilities. Where platforms are accessible, learners can complete tasks—such as accessing course notes, submitting assignments, or participating in discussions—without constant reliance on peers or staff. This is particularly important in higher education, where autonomy is a key expectation of academic participation.
2.3.2 Accessibility versus Usability
Although related, accessibility and usability are not the same. Usability refers to how efficiently and satisfactorily a system can be used by the general population. Accessibility focuses specifically on whether users with disabilities can use the system at all, using assistive technologies and alternative interaction methods. A platform may appear usable to many users while remaining inaccessible to students who depend on screen readers, captions, keyboard navigation, or simplified layouts. Effective e-learning systems therefore require both high usability and intentional accessibility design (W3C, 2018).
2.3.3 Accessibility as a Quality Requirement in System Design
From a system design perspective, accessibility is best treated as a core quality attribute alongside security, reliability, and performance. When accessibility is delayed until after implementation, it becomes more costly and difficult to address, often leading to partial fixes rather than systematic inclusion. By contrast, accessibility-by-design approaches embed requirements such as semantic structure, keyboard operability, caption support, and user customization from the start, improving the overall quality of the platform for diverse learners (W3C, 2018; UNESCO, 2020).
2.3.4 Digital Accessibility in Education
The importance of digital accessibility in education is rooted in its capacity to promote equity, independence, and meaningful participation for all learners. When digital learning environments are accessible, students with disabilities are empowered to engage with academic content without constant dependence on external assistance from peers or institutional staff. Research demonstrates that accessibility enhances not only inclusion but also overall system quality, as accessible platforms tend to be clearer, more structured, and easier to use for all learners (Kelly et al., 2020).
Digital accessibility is closely associated with the concept of digital equity, which emphasizes fair and just access to technological resources and opportunities. In higher education, digital equity requires that students are not disadvantaged due to physical, sensory, or cognitive differences when interacting with learning platforms. Inaccessible systems contribute to a digital divide in which students with disabilities experience systematic exclusion from academic participation, assessment activities, and collaborative learning opportunities (Watkins, 2019).
From a pedagogical perspective, inaccessible e-learning systems can negatively affect student motivation, confidence, and retention. Learners who repeatedly encounter barriers such as unreadable content, inaccessible navigation, or time-restricted assessments may experience frustration and disengagement. Studies suggest that these barriers can lead to reduced academic performance and increased withdrawal rates among students with disabilities (Burgstahler, 2015).
Conversely, accessible digital learning environments support autonomy and dignity by allowing students to control how they interact with content. Features such as adjustable text size, customizable color contrast, keyboard operability, consistent navigation structures, and flexible assessment timing enhance usability and comprehension. These features also benefit students without disabilities, reinforcing the argument that accessibility improvements often produce universal benefits across the learner population.
Accessibility should therefore be regarded as a fundamental quality attribute of educational technology rather than an optional enhancement. Treating accessibility as a core requirement aligns digital learning systems with inclusive education principles and ensures compliance with international standards.
Institutions that adopt accessibility-by-design approaches are better positioned to support diverse learners and fulfill ethical and legal responsibilities.
2.4 Categories of Disabilities and Digital Learning Needs
Students with disabilities represent a diverse population with varying needs and interaction requirements. Disabilities differ in nature, severity, and impact on learning, making it essential for
e-learning systems to provide flexible and adaptive support mechanisms. Understanding the digital learning needs associated with different disability categories enables system designers to anticipate barriers and incorporate inclusive solutions proactively (WHO, 2022).
Disabilities are commonly classified into visual, hearing, motor or physical, and cognitive or learning disabilities. Each category presents unique challenges in digital learning environments and requires specific accessibility considerations. Designing a single platform that accommodates these diverse needs requires adherence to inclusive design principles and international accessibility standards.
2.4.1 Visual Impairments
Visual impairments include total blindness, low vision, and color vision deficiencies. Students with visual impairments often rely on assistive technologies such as screen readers, screen magnifiers, and braille displays to access digital content. For these technologies to function effectively, e-learning platforms must provide well-structured content with semantic HTML markup, descriptive alternative text for images, and logical heading hierarchies (Lazar et al., 2017).
Color contrast and visual presentation also play a critical role for learners with low vision. Insufficient contrast between foreground and background elements can render content unreadable, while reliance on color alone to convey meaning may exclude users with color blindness. Accessible platforms should therefore meet WCAG contrast requirements and provide non-color indicators such as text labels or patterns.
Inaccessible visual design can significantly hinder academic participation by making navigation difficult and increasing cognitive effort. Ensuring visual accessibility improves readability, supports efficient navigation, and enhances overall learning effectiveness for visually impaired students.
2.4.2 Hearing Impairments
Hearing impairments range from mild hearing loss to complete deafness. In digital learning environments, students with hearing impairments encounter barriers when instructional content relies heavily on audio elements without appropriate alternatives. Video lectures, podcasts, and audio instructions that lack captions or transcripts exclude deaf and hard-of-hearing learners from accessing critical learning information (Fichten et al., 2019).
Accessible e-learning platforms should provide synchronized captions and accurate transcripts for all multimedia content. Where feasible, sign language interpretations may further enhance accessibility. Visual alerts and text-based notifications also ensure that system messages and announcements are communicated effectively to hearing-impaired users.
Incorporating multiple modalities for information delivery supports inclusive learning and aligns with Universal Design for Learning principles. Such approaches benefit not only hearing-impaired students but also learners in noisy environments or those who prefer text-based learning materials.
2.4.3 Motor and Physical Disabilities
Motor or physical disabilities affect an individual’s ability to perform physical interactions such as typing, clicking, or navigating interfaces using a mouse. Conditions such as cerebral palsy, spinal cord injuries, arthritis, and limb loss may limit fine motor control. Students with motor impairments often rely on alternative input devices, including keyboard-only navigation, voice recognition software, eye-tracking systems, or adaptive switches (Shinohara & Wobbrock, 2011).
E-learning platforms that depend on precise mouse movements, drag-and-drop interactions, or time-sensitive actions can present significant challenges for these learners. Accessible design principles emphasize full keyboard operability, adjustable time limits, and simple interaction mechanisms that reduce physical effort.
Designing for motor accessibility improves usability not only for students with permanent disabilities but also for users with temporary impairments or situational limitations, such as injuries or limited device access. This reinforces the inclusive value of accessibility-oriented design.
2.4.4 Cognitive and Learning Disabilities
Cognitive and learning disabilities include conditions such as dyslexia, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD), autism spectrum disorders, and memory impairments. These disabilities may affect information processing, concentration, comprehension, and retention. In digital learning contexts, overly complex layouts, excessive animations, inconsistent navigation, and dense text can overwhelm learners and hinder effective learning (Sweller, 2019).
Accessible e-learning platforms should prioritize clarity, predictability, and simplicity. Features such as clear navigation structures, chunked content presentation, readable fonts, and the ability to control multimedia playback support learners with cognitive disabilities. Reducing cognitive load enhances comprehension and engagement across diverse learner groups.
Providing multiple ways to access and engage with content aligns with Universal Design for Learning principles and promotes inclusive learning experiences. These design strategies support learners with cognitive disabilities while also benefiting students with different learning preferences and needs.
2.5 Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)
The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are internationally recognized standards developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) to guide the creation of accessible web content for persons with disabilities. WCAG is widely adopted across sectors—including government, health, commerce, and education—because it provides a clear technical pathway for ensuring that digital platforms can be used by people with diverse abilities (W3C, 2018). In e-learning, WCAG is particularly relevant because learning activities are delivered through web interfaces that students must access frequently for reading, assessments, submission of assignments, and communication.
2.5.1 WCAG as a Standard for Inclusive Digital Learning
In educational environments, accessibility is closely tied to fairness and academic participation. A platform may appear functional for many users, yet be effectively unusable for a student who depends on a screen reader, keyboard navigation, captions, or simplified layouts. WCAG provides requirements that help developers and institutions reduce such barriers. For example, the guideline on text alternatives ensures that images used in lecture notes—such as diagrams, charts, or screenshots—can be understood by students who cannot see them. Similarly, multimedia guidelines require captions and transcripts to support learners who cannot access audio content.
In practice, WCAG is valuable to e-learning because it translates inclusive education principles into implementable design and development tasks. In design-and-implementation projects, WCAG also serves as a measurable benchmark for evaluation: system features can be assessed against WCAG success criteria to determine whether accessibility goals have been met.
2.5.2 Structure of WCAG: Principles, Guidelines, and Success Criteria
WCAG is organized hierarchically. At the top are four accessibility principles known as POUR: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust. Each principle contains guidelines, and each guideline includes testable success criteria. This structure makes WCAG useful for both planning and auditing. Instead of treating accessibility as a vague goal, WCAG provides specific requirements such as “provide text alternatives,” “ensure keyboard access,” and “make content readable and predictable” (W3C, 2018).
2.5.3 Perceivable Principle
The perceivable principle requires that information and user interface components must be presented in ways users can perceive. For e-learning platforms, this includes providing text alternatives (alt text) for images and icons, ensuring that information is not communicated by color alone, and supporting content that can be re-sized without loss of functionality. Perceivable design also includes accessible multimedia—captions for videos, transcripts for audio, and in some cases audio descriptions for visual-only content. These provisions are essential for students with visual or hearing impairments and also support learners in low-bandwidth settings where video may not load reliably.
A common issue in learning portals is the use of scanned PDF lecture notes that are not text-selectable. Where notes are uploaded as images, screen readers cannot interpret them without optical character recognition, which may introduce errors. Perceivable accessibility therefore encourages institutions to provide true text documents or properly tagged PDFs that preserve structure and readability.
2.5.4 Operable Principle
The operable principle requires that users can operate the interface regardless of their method of interaction. In e-learning systems, operability includes full keyboard navigation, visible focus indicators, logical tab order, and avoidance of interaction patterns that demand precise mouse movements. It also includes providing enough time for tasks and avoiding flashing content that may trigger seizures.
Operable design is particularly important for students with motor impairments who may use keyboard-only navigation, switch devices, or voice control tools.
In assessment contexts, operability also relates to how quizzes and assignments are structured. If timed tests cannot be adjusted or if question navigation is confusing, students who require assistive input methods may be disadvantaged. Accessible e-learning systems therefore often include flexible timing and clear, consistent navigation patterns that reduce interaction burden.
2.5.5 Understandable Principle
The understandable principle focuses on ensuring that content and interface behavior are easy to comprehend. For learners with cognitive and learning disabilities, unclear language, inconsistent page layouts, unexpected pop-ups, or unpredictable navigation can create confusion and increase cognitive load. Understandable design encourages readable text, consistent menus, clear instructions, and predictable system behavior. It also supports error prevention and recovery—such as clear form validation messages during registration or assignment submission.
In e-learning platforms, this principle is also relevant to the design of learning content. Breaking long materials into manageable sections, providing clear headings, and using consistent formatting supports comprehension. When combined with accessibility features such as adjustable font size and spacing, understandable design can significantly improve learning outcomes for a broad range of students.
2.5.6 Robust Principle
The robust principle requires that content remains compatible with a wide range of user agents, including assistive technologies. Robustness is closely tied to proper coding practices—valid HTML, semantic structure, and appropriate use of ARIA (Accessible Rich Internet Applications) attributes where necessary. Inaccessible coding patterns, such as unlabeled form inputs or non-semantic clickable elements, can cause assistive technologies to misinterpret content or fail entirely. Robust platforms are more likely to remain accessible across different browsers, devices, and future technology updates (W3C, 2018).
2.5.7 Conformance Levels (A, AA, AAA) and Their Implications
WCAG defines three conformance levels: A (minimum), AA (intermediate), and AAA (highest). Many institutions aim for Level AA because it addresses the most common barriers while remaining practical to implement. Level AA includes requirements such as sufficient color contrast, captions for prerecorded videos, and clear focus indicators for keyboard navigation. Level AAA provides additional enhancements but may be difficult to achieve consistently across all content types. In this study, WCAG
2.1 Level AA is considered appropriate for an institutional e-learning platform because it balances inclusivity with feasibility.
2.5.8 Accessibility Evaluation Approaches for WCAG Compliance
WCAG compliance is typically assessed through a combination of automated and manual evaluation. Automated tools can detect issues such as missing alt text, insufficient contrast, and missing form labels. However, many accessibility problems require manual testing—for example, assessing whether headings are meaningful, whether keyboard navigation is logical, and whether screen-reader announcements make sense. In e-learning contexts, evaluation should also include testing with realistic user tasks such as logging in, downloading lecture notes, attempting quizzes, submitting assignments, and participating in discussions.
2.6 Assistive Technologies in E-Learning
Assistive technologies (AT) refer to devices, software, and tools that increase, maintain, or improve the functional capabilities of individuals with disabilities. In e-learning environments, AT enables students with disabilities to access course materials, navigate interfaces, communicate, and complete assessments. The effectiveness of AT depends on the accessibility of the platform itself; where platforms are poorly structured, even advanced AT may not work effectively (Burgstahler, 2015).
2.6.1 Screen Readers and Braille Technologies
Screen readers are among the most widely used AT tools for students with visual impairments. They convert on-screen text and interface information into synthetic speech or braille output. Common screen readers include JAWS and NVDA for Windows and VoiceOver for Apple devices. For screen readers to function properly, e-learning platforms must provide semantic headings, labeled buttons and form fields, meaningful link text, and properly tagged documents. Where these are missing, screen readers may announce content inaccurately, making navigation slow and frustrating.
Refreshable braille displays complement screen readers by translating text into braille output. These devices are valuable for learners who prefer tactile reading. However, braille access is also dependent on the platform’s structure. For instance, if a page is built with non-semantic elements, braille users may struggle to locate headings, menus, or interactive controls.
2.6.2 Screen Magnifiers and Visual Enhancement Tools
Students with low vision may rely on screen magnifiers and visual enhancement tools that enlarge content or adjust contrast. Examples include ZoomText and built-in browser zoom features. Platforms that do not support responsive design may break when zoomed, causing overlapping text or hidden buttons. Accessible e-learning platforms should therefore support reflow, flexible layouts, and clear typography to ensure usability under magnification.
2.6.3 Speech Recognition and Speech-to-Text Tools
Speech recognition tools support students with motor impairments by allowing them to navigate interfaces and input text using voice commands. These tools include speech-to-text dictation for writing and voice commands for navigation. For effective use, platforms should provide clear focus states, predictable navigation, and accessible form controls. Where interactive elements are not labeled properly, voice systems may not reliably identify clickable targets.
2.6.4 Alternative Input Devices and Switch Access
Alternative input devices include adaptive keyboards, joystick mice, switch controls, sip-and-puff systems, and eye-tracking devices. These tools enable users with limited motor control to interact with digital systems. E-learning platforms that rely on drag-and-drop interactions or complex gestures can be inaccessible to such users. Designing for simple, discrete input actions—such as clickable buttons and keyboard shortcuts—supports wider participation.
2.6.5 Captioning, Transcription, and Sign-Language Supports
For students with hearing impairments, captioning and transcription tools are essential. Captions provide synchronized text for spoken content, while transcripts offer full text versions of audio materials. Accessible e-learning platforms should ensure that media players support captions, that captions are accurate, and that transcripts are easy to locate. Where possible, sign-language interpretation provides additional inclusion for users who prefer sign language as a primary mode of communication.
2.6.6 Common Compatibility Challenges in Practice
Although AT tools can be powerful, real-world use often reveals compatibility challenges. Examples include documents uploaded as scanned images, video content without captions, interactive elements
that are not keyboard accessible, and dynamic web components that do not announce updates to screen readers. These challenges reinforce the need to build e-learning systems with accessibility as a baseline requirement. When platforms comply with standards such as WCAG and follow semantic development practices, AT integration becomes more reliable and user experience improves.
In contexts such as Nigeria, cost and availability of AT tools may also affect adoption. Therefore, accessible platforms should aim to work effectively with free or widely available tools, such as NVDA and built-in operating system accessibility features, while minimizing bandwidth demands to support users with limited internet access.
2.7 Accessible E-Learning in the Nigerian Context
The adoption of e-learning technologies in Nigeria has increased steadily over the past decade as a result of growing internet penetration, widespread mobile phone usage, and the need to expand access to higher education. Universities and other tertiary institutions have adopted learning management systems to support teaching, learning, and assessment activities. This trend intensified during the COVID-19 pandemic, when physical campuses were closed and institutions were compelled to adopt remote instructional methods (Federal Ministry of Education, 2021).
Although the expansion of e-learning represents a positive development, its benefits are not equally experienced by all students. For students with disabilities, online learning environments often replicate or even intensify barriers present in traditional classrooms. Many institutional platforms are designed for the general student population, with limited consideration for accessibility requirements. Consequently, students with visual, hearing, motor, and cognitive impairments encounter obstacles that restrict participation in lectures, assessments, and collaborative learning activities (Akinsola & Adeyemi, 2020).
Accessibility challenges in Nigerian e-learning systems are partly linked to limited awareness of digital accessibility standards among developers, instructors, and institutional administrators. Accessibility is frequently treated as an optional feature rather than a core system requirement. As a result, accommodations are often reactive and informal, relying on individual lecturers or disability support units rather than systematic institution-wide policies.
From a policy perspective, Nigeria has established legal and ethical frameworks that support inclusive education. The National Policy on Education emphasizes equal access to educational opportunities, while the Discrimination Against Persons with Disabilities (Prohibition) Act mandates the removal of barriers faced by persons with disabilities in public institutions. Despite these provisions, enforcement within digital learning environments remains weak due to funding constraints, limited technical expertise, and insufficient monitoring mechanisms.
Infrastructure limitations further compound accessibility issues. Unreliable electricity supply, inconsistent internet connectivity, and the high cost of data restrict sustained participation in online learning. For students with disabilities, these challenges are intensified by the cost and limited availability of assistive technologies. These realities highlight the importance of designing e-learning platforms that function effectively in low-bandwidth environments and are compatible with free or low-cost assistive tools.
Despite these challenges, opportunities exist for improving accessible e-learning in Nigeria. Growing advocacy for disability inclusion, increasing awareness of accessibility standards, and the availability of open-source technologies provide a foundation for context-appropriate solutions. Institutional commitment, capacity building, and inclusive system design can enable universities such as the University of Ilorin to deliver more equitable digital learning experiences.
2.8 Theoretical Framework
Theoretical frameworks provide structured perspectives that support understanding, design, and evaluation of information systems. In this study, the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and the Social Model of Disability are adopted to explain how accessibility influences user adoption, usability, and participation in e-learning environments. Together, these theories provide complementary insights into both technology use and inclusive design.
2.8.1 Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), developed by Davis (1989), explains user acceptance of technology through two core constructs: Perceived Usefulness (PU) and Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU). PU refers to the degree to which a user believes that using a system enhances performance, while PEOU describes the extent to which the system is perceived as easy to use. These constructs influence users’ attitudes, intentions, and actual system usage.
In accessible e-learning contexts, TAM highlights the role of accessibility as a critical determinant of perceived ease of use. For students with disabilities, inaccessible navigation, unreadable content, or incompatibility with assistive technologies increases effort and frustration. Even if a system is academically beneficial, such barriers can result in negative perceptions and eventual rejection of the platform. Accessibility therefore directly influences both perceived ease of use and perceived usefulness among students with disabilities.
By applying TAM, this study emphasizes that accessible design is not only an ethical requirement but also a practical strategy for improving system adoption and sustained usage. Platforms that are easy to navigate, compatible with assistive tools, and flexible in interaction are more likely to be accepted by diverse learners, leading to improved engagement and learning outcomes.
2.8.2 Social Model of Disability
The Social Model of Disability re-frames disability as a consequence of environmental and societal barriers rather than individual impairments. According to this model, individuals are disabled not by their physical or cognitive conditions but by systems and environments that fail to accommodate diversity (Oliver, 1990). In digital learning environments, inaccessible platforms function as disabling structures that limit participation.
Applying the Social Model of Disability to e-learning emphasizes institutional responsibility for removing digital barriers. Inaccessible interfaces, non-captioned videos, and keyboard-inaccessible assessments are examples of design choices that exclude students with disabilities. By contrast, inclusive design practices transform digital platforms into enabling environments that support equal participation and academic success.
The Social Model aligns closely with the objectives of this study, which seeks to design an e-learning platform that removes digital barriers rather than placing responsibility on individual students to adapt. Combined with TAM, this framework supports a holistic understanding of accessibility as both a social obligation and a determinant of technology acceptance.
2.9 Review of Related Works
The review of related works is a critical component of a design and implementation study because it situates the proposed system within the context of existing research and technological solutions. In accessible e-learning research, related works provide insight into how accessibility challenges have been conceptualized, the types of solutions that have been proposed, and the limitations that persist in practice. This section therefore reviews academic studies, existing learning management systems, and accessibility-focused platforms relevant to the design of an accessible e-learning system.
Existing studies on accessible e-learning generally fall into three broad categories: evaluations of mainstream learning management systems, development of prototype accessibility tools, and conceptual studies proposing accessibility frameworks. While these works contribute valuable knowledge, they often address accessibility in fragmented ways, focusing on specific disabilities or isolated system components rather than holistic platform design.
2.9.1 Studies on Accessibility of Learning Management Systems
Several researchers have examined the accessibility of widely used learning management systems such as Moodle, Blackboard, and Canvas. Seale (2014) conducted an extensive study on the accessibility of mainstream LMS platforms and concluded that although accessibility features are increasingly available, their implementation is inconsistent. Many platforms technically support accessibility standards, yet fail to deliver accessible learning experiences due to poor configuration, lack of instructor awareness, and limited institutional enforcement.
Al-Azawei et al. (2017) investigated the adoption of e-learning systems among students with disabilities and found that most platforms prioritize general usability over accessibility. Their findings indicate that students with disabilities often experience frustration and reduced engagement because accessibility needs are not considered during initial system design. This reinforces the argument that accessibility must be embedded at the design stage rather than retrofitted after deployment.
Research focusing on Moodle highlights similar challenges. While Moodle includes accessibility features such as keyboard navigation support and compatibility with screen readers, effective accessibility depends heavily on how instructors upload and structure content. Kelly et al. (2020) observe that lecture materials uploaded as scanned images, poorly structured documents, or uncaptioned videos undermine Moodle’s accessibility capabilities. As a result, accessibility becomes optional rather than guaranteed.
2.9.2 Disability-Specific Accessibility Solutions
Some related works focus on accessibility solutions designed for specific disability groups. For example, several systems prioritize visual accessibility by improving screen-reader compatibility or providing text-to-speech support. While these approaches enhance access for visually impaired learners, they often neglect the needs of students with motor, hearing, or cognitive disabilities. This single-disability focus limits the overall inclusiveness of such systems.
Other studies have proposed captioning and transcription tools to support students with hearing impairments. These tools improve access to video and audio content but may not address broader interaction challenges such as navigation complexity or inaccessible assessments. Consequently, students with multiple disabilities may continue to face barriers even when some accessibility features are present.
2.9.3 Conceptual Frameworks and Accessibility Models
Beyond system evaluations, some studies propose conceptual frameworks for accessible e-learning. These frameworks often draw on Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles, emphasizing multiple means of representation, engagement, and expression. While such frameworks provide useful guidance, they are frequently abstract and lack concrete implementation details that developers can translate into functional systems.
Other models emphasize compliance with accessibility standards such as WCAG. Although standards-based approaches provide clear technical requirements, they may not fully addresscontextual challenges such as low-bandwidth environments, limited access to assistive technologies, or instructor practices in developing countries. This gap highlights the need for context-aware design approaches.
2.9.4 Analysis of Related Works
Espada-Chavarria, R., et al. (2023): Explores Universal Design for Learning (UDL) in higher education, arguing that "one-size-fits-all" digital instruction fails students with disabilities and that platforms must offer flexible representation to ensure equity.
Garrad, T.-A., & Nolan, H. (2022): Investigates the impact of proactive unit design, demonstrating that embedding accessibility features during the initial development phase (rather than as an afterthought) significantly improves engagement and reduces student attrition.
Baguma, R., & Wolters, M. K. (2021): Focuses on the implementation of Virtual Learning Environments (VLEs), noting that even if a platform like Moodle is technically accessible, it remains a barrier if instructors do not format the actual learning content correctly.
Yuwono, J., et al. (2022): Analyzes the challenges of emergency remote teaching, identifying that a lack of instructor awareness and specialized pedagogical tools were the primary hurdles for disabled students during the rapid shift to online learning.
Huff Jr., E. W. (2022): Details the design of an Accessible Learning Content Management System (ALCMS) specifically for blind and visually impaired (BVI) users, emphasizing screen-reader optimization and simplified navigation architecture.
Joy, S., & Gundmi, A. (2024): Examines e-learning for hearing-impaired students, highlighting that common collaborative tools often lack the integrated sign-language support and visual aids necessary for effective digital inclusion.
AccessiLearnAI (2025/2026): Highlights the modern shift toward AI-driven personalization, where microservices are used to automate text simplification and provide real-time accessibility adjustments based on individual user profiles.

Identified Gaps and How This Study Responds
Overall, the comparative analysis reveals a persistent gap between accessibility principles and platform-level execution. Related works confirm that accessibility is too often treated as an add-on rather than a system requirement, leading to partial compliance and continued exclusion. In addition, many solutions assume stable infrastructure, reliable bandwidth, and widespread access to assistive technologies—assumptions that may not hold in many developing contexts.
This study addresses the identified gaps by designing an accessible e-learning platform that (i) supports multiple disability categories within one unified system, (ii) aligns with WCAG-guided accessibility at interface and content levels, (iii) embeds enforcement mechanisms (e.g., guided uploads, content validation, accessibility prompts/checks) so accessibility is consistent across courses, and (iv) considers contextual constraints such as varying device capabilities and bandwidth conditions. By shifting accessibility from “optional best practice” to “built-in system behavior,” the proposed platform aims to improve independent participation and equitable learning outcomes for students with disabilities.

This chapter reviewed extensive literature on e-learning systems, digital accessibility, disability inclusion, and accessible system design. The review covered the evolution and role of e-learning in higher education, the importance of accessibility in digital learning environments, categories of disabilities and their learning requirements, web accessibility standards, assistive technologies, the Nigerian context, relevant theoretical frameworks, and existing related works.
The literature demonstrates that while e-learning technologies have expanded educational access, students with disabilities continue to face systemic barriers due to inaccessible platform design and inconsistent implementation of accessibility standards. Existing solutions often address isolated problems rather than providing comprehensive, inclusive platforms.
These identified gaps justify the design and implementation of an accessible e-learning platform that integrates international standards with context-aware design considerations. The insights gained from this literature review inform the system requirements, architectural decisions, and evaluation criteria discussed in the next chapter, which focuses on research methodology and system design.
