ABSTRACT 
The rapid growth in smartphone ownership has transformed how students manage academic 
responsibilities. This study focuses on the design and implementation of a mobile application for 
academic scheduling and reminders tailored for students of the Universit y of Ilorin. The 
application integrates lecture timetables, assignments, examinations, and personal study plans into 
a unified digital platform. The methodology adopted includes system analysis, rapid application 
development (RAD), and iterative prototypin g, ensuring a user -centered design aligned with 
students’ academic workflows. Core functionalities include task scheduling, automated reminders, 
notifications, and visual progress tracking. Evaluation of the system involves usability testing, 
functional te sting, and effectiveness assessment among selected university students. Findings 
indicate that the application improves time management, task adherence, and organization, while 
reducing missed deadlines and cognitive overload associated with fragmented planning tools. The 
study concludes that a dedicated academic scheduling mobile application enhances students’ 
productivity, supports self-regulated learning, and provides a practical solution to the limitations 
of generic productivity applications in higher education settings. Recommendations include 
further integration with institutional platforms and expansion to additional academic support 
features.

CHAPTER ONE 
INTRODUCTION 
1.1 Background to the Study 
The rapid growth in smartphone ownership and mobile computing has significantly changed how 
students manage their daily and academic responsibilities. Mobile devices are now constantly 
available, portable, and capable of running sophisticated applications that support planning, 
communication, and information management. As a result, mobile applications have become 
practical tools for organizing schedules, tracking tasks, and receiving reminders, making them 
particularly useful within educational settings. R ecent studies indicate that mobile learning and 
productivity applications can improve students’ organization, engagement, and consistency in 
completing academic tasks when compared with traditional manual planning methods (Ally & 
Wark, 2020; Lazar, Feng, & Hochheiser, 2021). 
 
Academic scheduling applications are developed specifically to assist students in coordinating 
lectures, tutorials, assignments, examinations, and personal study sessions. Reminder systems 
complement these functions by providing timely alerts that prompt students to act before deadlines 
occur. Together, scheduling and reminder features support better time allocation, reduce 
forgetfulness, and promote self -regulated learning behaviours . Research in mobile learning 
environments shows that digital planning tools help students monitor their progress, set priorities, 
and manage competing academic demands more effectively (Broadbent & Poon, 2021). 
 
Despite these benefits, many existing mobile scheduling tools remain general-purpose rather than 
academically focused. Popular platforms such as Google Calendar, Apple Reminders, and generic 
to-do list applications are designed for everyday productivity an d often lack features required for 
structured academic planning. For example, they rarely provide semester -based organization, 
course-linked task grouping, assessment tracking, or workload visualization across an academic 
term. Consequently, students must manually adapt these tools, which can be time-consuming and 
prone to error. 
 
Furthermore, several existing applications present usability and accessibility challenges. Complex 
interfaces, excessive features, subscription -based models, and reliance on constant internet 
connectivity can discourage sustained use. These issues are more  pronounced in developing

regions where students may experience unstable network access or limited financial resources. 
Recent usability studies emphasize that students prefer simple, lightweight, and offline -capable 
academic tools that directly reflect their educational workflows rather than feature-heavy general 
planners (Alvarez & Kim, 2020; Lazar et al., 2021). 
 
Another limitation of current platforms is the fragmentation of academic planning activities. 
Students often use separate tools for timetables, notes, reminders, and assignment tracking. 
Managing multiple applications increases cognitive load and reduces e fficiency, making it easier 
to overlook important deadlines or duplicate tasks. This lack of integration highlights the need for 
a unified system that consolidates all academic responsibilities into a single environment. 
 
At the same time, advancements in cross-platform mobile development frameworks have made it 
easier to design affordable and accessible applications tailored to specific user groups. These 
technologies enable the creation of customized solutions that operat e efficiently across different 
devices while supporting offline functionality and intuitive interfaces. Such capabilities create an 
opportunity to develop a dedicated academic scheduling and reminder application that directly 
addresses the unique needs of university students. 
 
In view of these considerations, this study intends to design and implement a mobile application 
for academic scheduling and reminders for students of the University of Ilorin, Kwara State, 
Nigeria. The proposed system aims to integrate lecture timetables, assignments, examinations, and 
study plans into a single platform. By provid ing structured organization and timely notifications, 
the application is expected to reduce missed deadlines, improve time management, and enhance 
students’ overall academic productivity.

date-based reminders. They rarely incorporate academic-oriented features such as semester-based 
scheduling, course-linked task gr ouping, assessment tracking, priority weighting, or structured 
timelines that reflect the cyclical nature of university coursework. Consequently, these applications

2.1 Introduction 
This chapter presents a review of related literature on academic scheduling, time management, and 
the use of mobile reminder applications in higher education. The review examines key theoretical 
perspectives, conceptual frameworks, and empirical studies relevant to time management practices 
and mobile-based academic planning tools. Emphasis is placed on understanding how students 
organize academic activities, the challenges associated with ineffective time management, and the 
role of digital technologies in supporting academic organization. 
 
In addition, this chapter explores existing mobile scheduling and reminder systems, highlighting 
their strengths and limitations within academic contexts. The purpose of the review is to establish 
a theoretical foundation for the study, identify gaps in ex isting academic planning solutions, and 
justify the need for a dedicated mobile application designed specifically to address the scheduling 
and reminder needs of university students. 
 
2.2 Conceptual Review  
2.1.1 Mobile Applications in Education 
Mobile applications have become an integral part of modern educational environments due to the 
widespread ownership and use of smartphones among students. The portability and constant 
availability of mobile devices allow learners to access educational tool s and resources beyond 
traditional classroom settings. According to Traxler (2018), mobile learning technologies promote 
flexibility by enabling students to engage with learning materials anytime and anywhere, thereby 
supporting informal and self-directed learning.

Educational mobile applications serve diverse functions, ranging from content delivery and 
assessment to collaboration and academic support. These applications have been shown to enhance 
learner engagement by providing interactive features, multimedia cont ent, and personalized 
experiences tailored to individual learning needs. Research indicates that mobile learning 
environments can foster learner autonomy by allowing students to control the pace, timing, and 
structure of their learning activities (Ally & Wark, 2020). 
 
In addition to instructional content, many mobile applications focus on supporting students’ 
academic behaviors and organizational skills. Scheduling and reminder applications represent an 
important subset of educational mobile tools that prioritize academ ic organization rather than 
direct content instruction. These tools assist students in managing time, organizing tasks, and 
tracking academic commitments, which are essential components of effective learning. 
 
2.1.2 Academic Scheduling and Reminder Systems for University Students 
Academic scheduling systems are designed to help students organize academic activities such as 
courses, assignment deadlines, examinations, and personal study sessions. These systems provide 
structured frameworks that enable students to plan tasks in advance, allocate time appropriately, 
and maintain an overview of academic responsibilities. Reminder systems function as a 
complementary component by delivering alerts and notifications that prompt students to take 
action at the appropriate time. 
 
Research has consistently demonstrated the effectiveness of reminder technologies in supporting 
task completion. Sellen, Louie, Harris, and Wilkins (1997) found that reminder systems 
significantly reduce the incidence of forgotten tasks by providing timely  cues that reinforce 
planned activities. By improving task recall, reminder systems help students adhere to schedules 
and maintain consistency in task execution. 
 
Scheduling and reminder applications support academic performance by offloading memory -
intensive tasks through automated notifications and visual representations of schedules. By 
externalizing memory demands, such applications help students remember import ant deadlines, 
classes, and assessments without excessive cognitive effort. Kim, Kim, and Wachter (2013) note

9 
 
that reminder -based mobile tools improve task awareness and reduce forgetfulness, thereby 
supporting consistent academic engagement. 
 
In academic contexts, scheduling and reminder systems are particularly important due to the 
repetitive and deadline-driven nature of student workloads. Students are often required to manage 
multiple courses with overlapping assessments and varying submissi on timelines. Without 
effective scheduling support, students may struggle to track deadlines and distribute workload 
evenly across academic periods. 
 
Despite their benefits, many existing scheduling and reminder tools remain generic in design and 
do not fully address the specific needs of students. Generic tools often lack features such as 
semester-based timelines, course -specific task grouping, assignment prioritization based on 
grading weight, and structured exam preparation reminders. As a result, they fail to align with the 
organizational patterns required in higher education settings. This limitation underscores the need 
for specialized academic scheduling applications tailored to university students’ workflows and 
academic demands. 
 
2.1.1 Time Management in Academic Settings 
Time management refers to how individuals plan, allocate, and regulate their time to achieve 
academic goals efficiently. In higher education, effective time management involves organizing 
study schedules, attending lectures, completing assignments, prepari ng for examinations, and 
balancing academic responsibilities with personal and social activities. Recent research confirms 
that strong time management skills are associated with improved academic performance, reduced 
stress, and enhanced overall learning e xperiences among university students ( Alabdullatif et al., 
2022; Krajewski et al., 2023). 
 
Students who exhibit effective time management are more likely to engage in purposeful planning, 
goal setting, prioritization, and self‑monitoring. These strategies enable learners to allocate 
appropriate time to academic tasks, monitor progress, and adjus t plans in response to changing 
demands. A study conducted by Gupta and Sehgal (2024) found that students with structured time 
management practices reported higher academic engagement and better control over their learning 
behaviors compared to peers without such skills.

Conversely, poor time management remains a significant challenge in higher education. 
Inadequate planning and ineffective time use contribute to academic procrastination, missed 
deadlines, increased stress, and lower academic satisfaction. Research by Liu and Wang (2024) 
highlights that procrastination often stems from difficulties in prioritizing tasks and unrealistic 
scheduling, which in turn negatively affects academic confidence and performance. 
 
The increasing complexity of university academic schedules characterized by overlapping courses, 
continuous assessments, and co‑curricular commitments further amplifies the need for structured 
time planning. Manual methods such as notebooks, physical planners, or fragmented digital tools 
are often insufficient in helping students manage multifaceted academic workloads effectively. As 
demands grow, technology‑supported planning tools have become essential for sustaining 
academic success and reducing cognitive load (Alshare & Miller, 2021; Attah et al., 2025). 
 
Mobile applications that integrate scheduling and reminder functionalities offer a promising 
solution to these challenges. By organizing academic events, deadlines, and study sessions in a 
centralized digital environment, such tools can support consistent planning and task monitoring. 
Recent studies demonstrate that mobile academic planners that provide clear visual timelines, 
customizable alerts, and course‑linked task grouping help students maintain structured time 
management and reduce instances of procrastination (Mohamed & Ali, 2023; Wang & Fang, 2025). 
 
Moreso, contemporary research underscores the importance of structured time management 
strategies for academic success and highlights the potential of mobile scheduling tools to support 
students in managing complex academic responsibilities.

application that is student‑centered, lightweight, and aligned with academic workflows to enhance 
organization, reduce cognitive overload, and improve academic outcomes.

3.3 Description of Existing System 
The existing academic scheduling and task management landscape is dominated by general -
purpose tools such as Google Calendar, Microsoft To Do, and Todoist. These applications provide 
basic functionality for creating events, setting deadlines, and receiving  reminders, but they are 
primarily designed for personal productivity rather than structured academic workflows. 
 
Current systems allow students to organize tasks and schedules on a day -to-day basis and set 
notifications for upcoming events. They offer features such as recurring events, priority labeling, 
and color -coded calendars. Users can generally view their tasks  in daily, weekly, or monthly 
formats and synchronize information across multiple devices. 
 
Despite these capabilities, existing systems have several deficiencies and limitations when applied 
to university academic needs: 
i. Lack of Academic Context: Most tools do not support semester-based planning, course-
linked task organization, or assessment tracking. Students must manually adapt these tools 
to fit their academic structure, which can be time-consuming and error-prone. 
ii. Fragmentation of Academic Planning: Students often need to use multiple applications 
simultaneously to manage lectures, assignments, exams, and personal study sessions. This

fragmentation increases cognitive load, duplication of effort, and the likelihood of missed 
deadlines. 
iii. Limited Reminder Customization: While reminders exist, many applications do not 
allow academic-specific notifications, such as reminders tied to assignment weight, exam 
preparation, or lecture conflicts. 
iv. Complexity and Usability Issues: Some tools include advanced features that are 
unnecessary for academic scheduling, creating steep learning curves and overwhelming 
interfaces. Others restrict key features behind subscriptions, limiting accessibility for 
students with financial constraints. 
v. Offline Functionality: Many applications require constant internet connectivity for full 
functionality. This can be a barrier for students in regions with unstable or limited internet 
access. 
vi. Minimal Integration of Personal Study Planning: Existing systems primarily focus on 
scheduling events and deadlines but do not support personalized study sessions or 
strategies for self-regulated learning. 
 
These deficiencies highlight the need for a dedicated academic mobile application that 
consolidates scheduling, reminders, and task management into a unified, user -friendly, and 
academically aligned platform. Such a system would directly address the limita tions of existing 
tools by providing course -specific planning, semester -long overviews, customizable reminders, 
offline capability, and simplified interfaces designed specifically for university students. 
 
3.4 Rapid Application Development Model 
Rapid Application Development (RAD) is a software development methodology that emphasizes 
iterative and incremental development, early delivery of functional components, and continuous 
user involvement. Unlike traditional linear models such as the Waterfal l model, RAD prioritizes 
frequent feedback, quick refinement, and adaptability to changing requirements throughout the 
development process (Dovetail, 2024; XB Software, 2024). 
 
A core principle of RAD is rapid prototyping, where components of the system are built quickly 
and iteratively refined based on stakeholder feedback. This process allows developers to validate 

--- Page 26 ---
17 
 
features early and adjust design decisions before full implementation, reducing the risk of 
misaligned specifications and costly redesigns at later stages (Dovetail, 2024; XB Software, 2024). 
 
RAD also fosters strong collaboration between developers and end users throughout the lifecycle 
of the system. By soliciting ongoing input from students ; the primary users of the academic 
scheduling application, the researcher who is the mobile application developer can ensure that the 
final product reflects actual user needs, aligns with academic workflows, and supports intuitive 
interaction. This user‑centered feedback loop is a key strength of RAD, leading to improved 
usability and higher satisfaction with the final system. 
 
Another advantage of the RAD approach is its flexibility in adapting to evolving requirements. 
Academic scheduling needs can vary by semester, course load, and personal study patterns, and 
RAD’s iterative cycles allow features to be refined continuously as  new insights emerge from 
testing and evaluation. As a result, the development process is responsive rather than rigid, 
enabling the system to remain aligned with user expectations (Dovetail, 2024). 
 
Component‑based development is another important feature of RAD. Modules such as scheduling, 
notifications, and task management can be designed, developed, and tested independently before 
integration. This modularity enhances reliability and simplifies deb ugging, allowing individual 
components to be optimized for performance and usability before they become part of the complete 
system (Dovetail, 2024). 
 
RAD’s emphasis on iterative feedback and modular construction also supports early identification 
and correction of usability issues. By involving students in regular review sessions, potential 
problems such as confusing navigation, inefficient workflows, or ineffective reminder settings can 
be identified and improved early, reducing the cost and effort of post‑release corrections (XB 
Software, 2024). 
 
Overall, RAD aligns well with the objectives of this study, which aims to deliver a mobile 
academic scheduling and reminder application that is functional, user‑friendly, and tailored to 
students’ academic needs. By facilitating continuous refinement, acti ve collaboration with end 
users, and flexible adaptation to changing requirements, RAD supports the development of a 
system that effectively enhances academic planning and time management.

4.3 System Architecture 
Software architecture represents the fundamental structure upon which a software system is 
designed and implemented. It describes how the various components of a software application 
interact with one another, how responsibilities are distributed across di fferent modules, and how 
information flows throughout the system. According to Pressman and Maxim (2020), software 
architecture serves as the blueprint of a software system by defining the relationships between its 
major components, interfaces, data struct ures, communication mechanisms, and deployment 
environment. Similarly, Sommerville (2021) explains that software architecture provides the 
organizational framework that guides software implementation, improves maintainability, 
enhances scalability, and simplifies future system evolution. 
A well-designed software architecture contributes significantly to software quality by promoting 
modularity, separation of concerns, reusability, fault isolation, maintainability, and scalability. 
Rather than implementing all functionalities within a singl e application layer, modern software 
systems divide responsibilities among independent modules that communicate through 
standardized interfaces. This architectural approach enables each component to evolve 
independently without significantly affecting other parts of the system. 
The Academic Companion for Education (ACE) was designed using a multi -tier client -server 
architecture. The system consists of three major layers that work together to deliver the 
application's functionality. 
These layers include: 
• Presentation Layer (Mobile Application)  
• Application Layer (Backend API)  
• Data Layer (Cloud Database)  
Each layer performs specialized responsibilities while communicating securely through RESTful 
web services over HTTPS. This separation of responsibilities follows the Separation of Concerns 
(SoC) principle, one of the fundamental principles of software eng ineering, which recommends 
dividing complex software systems into independent modules responsible for distinct 
functionalities (Pressman & Maxim, 2020). 

--- Page 51 ---
42 
 
Unlike traditional desktop applications where all processing occurs within a single executable 
program, the ACE system distributes computation across cloud infrastructure. The mobile 
application is responsible for user interaction and presentation, while t he backend processes 
requests, performs business logic, validates user input, enforces security rules, and communicates 
with the database. The database layer securely stores and retrieves persistent application data. 
This architectural approach provides several advantages over monolithic application designs. First, 
the separation of frontend, backend, and database responsibilities simplifies maintenance because 
updates can be applied to one layer without requiring sign ificant modifications to the others. 
Secondly, scalability is improved since backend services and database resources can be upgraded 
independently as the number of users increases. Thirdly, security is strengthened because sensitive 
operations such as auth entication, authorization, and data validation are performed on the server 
rather than on the client device. Finally, the architecture supports future extensibility by allowing 
new application modules and services to be integrated with minimal disruption t o existing 
functionality.

4.3.1 Overall Architectural Design 
The architectural design of the ACE application follows the client -server software architecture 
model. Client -server architecture is one of the most widely adopted architectural patterns in 
modern distributed systems because it separates user -facing applic ations from server -side 
processing and centralized data management (Sommerville, 2021). 
Within this model, the client is responsible for presenting information to users and collecting user 
input, while the server processes incoming requests, executes business logic, communicates with 
the database, and returns appropriate responses. 
In the context of the ACE application, the architecture consists of five major components: 
i. Mobile Client Application 
The mobile application serves as the primary interface through which students interact with the 
system. It provides features such as user registration, authentication, timetable management, 
assignment tracking, reminders, notifications, profile management,  and settings. The application 
was developed using React Native and the Expo framework to support both Android and iOS 
platforms from a single codebase. 
The client communicates exclusively with the backend through secure HTTPS requests. No direct 
communication occurs between the mobile application and the database, thereby improving system 
security and ensuring that all requests pass through the application's business logic layer. 
ii. Backend Application Server 
The backend serves as the central processing unit of the system. Developed using the NestJS  
framework, it implements the business rules governing the application, validates incoming 
requests, authenticates users, communicates with cloud services, and manages database operations. 

--- Page 55 ---
46 
 
The backend exposes RESTful API endpoints that receive requests from the mobile application, 
process the requested operations, and return standardized JSON responses. By centralizing 
business logic within the backend, consistency is maintained across all application modules while 
reducing code duplication and improving maintainability. 
iii. Cloud Database 
Persistent data generated by users is stored within a PostgreSQL relational database hosted on the 
Supabase cloud platform. The database stores information relating to user accounts, academic 
schedules, reminders, tasks, notifications, application settings, and authentication records. 
The relational nature of PostgreSQL enables efficient organization of related data while enforcing 
integrity through primary keys, foreign keys, indexing, constraints, and transactional consistency. 
Supabase additionally provides secure authentication services, row -level security policies, and 
managed database infrastructure, reducing administrative overhead while maintaining enterprise -
grade security. 
iv. Notification Service 
One of the major objectives of the ACE application is to remind students about upcoming academic 
activities. To achieve this objective, the system integrates Expo Notifications, which provides a 
unified interface for scheduling and delivering local and pus h notifications across supported 
mobile platforms. 
Notification requests originate from user -created schedules and reminders. The application 
calculates notification schedules based on the configured reminder settings before delivering alerts 
to the user's device at the appropriate time. 
This notification subsystem significantly enhances the application's usefulness by helping students 
remain aware of important academic events, deadlines, and scheduled learning activities. 
v. Web Landing Platform 
In addition to the mobile application, a lightweight web landing page was developed to provide 
information about the ACE application and facilitate user acquisition. The website introduces the 
application's objectives, highlights its major features, presents screenshots, and provides download 
instructions for prospective users.

appropriate service module. The service subsequently communicat es with the NestJS backend

accordingly. 
The layered organization adopted throughout the mobile application significantly improves

reliability, and ensure that the completed software performs according to user expectations before 
deployment. According to Pressman and Maxim (2020), software testing provides confidence that

reducing the likelihood of failures during practical use.

integrated into every stage of development using an iterative developmen t approach. Each newly

the cost associated with correcting defects at later stages of development.

correctly over the Internet.

5.4.1 Cloud Service Limitations 
One of the most significant challenges encountered during the implementation of the application 
involved the limitations imposed by free -tier cloud services. Since the project was developed as 
an academic project without dedicated financial sponsorship, several cloud platforms were initially 
utilized under their free usage plans. 
Although these services provided sufficient resources during the early stages of development, 
various operational restrictions became evident as the application evolved into a feature -rich 
academic management platform. 
For example, cloud platforms generally impose limits on storage capacity, network bandwidth, 
processing resources, concurrent connections, monthly build quotas, and application execution 
time. As the number of implemented features increased, these restrict ions occasionally affected 
development efficiency, testing activities, and deployment workflows. 
These limitations highlighted the practical trade -offs between free development resources and 
production-ready cloud infrastructure, emphasizing the importance of proper infrastructure 
planning during software engineering projects. 
5.4.2 Backend Hosting Constraints 
The backend services for the ACE application were deployed using the free tier of Render. 
Although Render provides an excellent environment for hosting Node.js applications with 
automated deployment, the free service plan automatically suspends inactive applications after a 
period of inactivity to conserve computing resources. 
Consequently, the first API request made after prolonged inactivity often experiences additional 
latency while the server instance is restarted. This behaviour, commonly referred to as cold start 
latency or server sleeping , temporarily increases response time for the initial request before 
normal performance resumes. 
During testing, this occasionally resulted in delayed authentication requests and slower loading of 
application data immediately after prolonged inactivity. Although this behaviour did not affect the 
correctness of application functionality, it slightly in fluenced the perceived responsiveness 
experienced by users during initial application startup.

Commercial hosting plans eliminate this limitation by maintaining continuously active server 
instances; however, these plans incur recurring operational costs that were beyond the scope of the 
present project. 
5.4.3 Mobile Application Build Restrictions 
Another significant challenge involved the generation of production -ready Android application 
packages. 
The application utilized Expo Application Services (EAS Build) to compile native Android builds 
through Expo's cloud infrastructure. While EAS significantly simplifies mobile application 
compilation by eliminating the need for local Android build environme nts, the free development 
plan imposes limitations on the number of cloud builds available within a given period. 
Because each production build consumes one build allocation, repeatedly generating new 
application packages after every minor software modification proved inefficient during 
development. 
Furthermore, cloud -based compilation introduces queue times that vary depending on server 
availability and the number of concurrent builds being processed by the platform. 
As development progressed, build queues occasionally increased the waiting time before newly 
generated application packages became available for testing. 
5.4.4 Application Update Management 
Closely related to build limitations was the challenge of efficiently distributing software updates 
during development and testing. 
Generating a completely new Android installation package after every modification would have 
significantly slowed the implementation process while consuming the available monthly cloud 
build allocation. 
To address this challenge, the project adopted Over-the-Air (OTA) updates using Expo Updates 
for modifications that affected JavaScript, TypeScript, styling, user interface behaviour, and 
application logic. 
OTA updates allowed compatible application changes to be delivered directly to installed devices 
without rebuilding the native Android application package. Native builds were therefore reserved

This approach significantly accelerated feature testing while improving development efficiency

5.4.5 Third-Party Service Dependency 
The architecture of the ACE application integrates multiple cloud services, including Supabase, 
Render, Vercel, Expo Application Services, Expo Notifications, and GitHub. 
While this distributed architecture improves scalability and maintainability, it also introduces 
dependencies on the availability and operational status of external cloud providers. 
Temporary service outages, scheduled maintenance periods, network interruptions, or changes to 
platform policies could potentially affect one or more application services despite the application's 
internal software functioning correctly. 
This dependency illustrates one of the practical considerations associated with cloud -native 
software development. 
5.4.6 Database Resource Constraints 
Supabase was selected as the cloud database platform because of its integrated PostgreSQL 
services, authentication system, storage capabilities, and developer-friendly APIs. 
However, the free service tier includes limitations relating to database storage capacity, file storage 
quotas, bandwidth allocation, and computational resources. 
Although these limitations were sufficient throughout the implementation and testing phases of 
this study, large -scale institutional deployment involving thousands of concurrent users would 
require migration to higher -capacity commercial service plans capa ble of supporting increased 
workloads.

5.5 Solutions Adopted 
To ensure the successful completion of the project despite the challenges encountered, several 
practical software engineering solutions were implemented throughout development. 
Rather than allowing infrastructure limitations to delay implementation, alternative approaches 
were adopted to optimize resource utilization while maintaining software quality. 
5.5.1 Adoption of Modular Software Architecture 
The application was implemented using a modular architecture provided by the NestJS framework 
together with organized React Native project structures. 
This modular organization simplified debugging, reduced software complexity, encouraged code 
reuse, and allowed individual application modules to be developed and tested independently. 
The resulting architecture also supports future expansion with minimal disruption to existing 
application functionality. 
5.5.2 Continuous Version Control 
Git and GitHub were employed throughout development to maintain complete source code history. 
Frequent commits ensured that implementation progress could be recovered whenever unexpected 
software issues occurred.

Version control also simplified collaboration, documentation, deployment automation, and source

5.5.3 Incremental Testing 
Rather than postponing software testing until implementation had been completed, continuous 
testing was performed after the implementation of each functional module. 
This incremental testing strategy significantly reduced debugging effort by allowing 
implementation errors to be identified immediately after introduction. 
5.5.4 Adoption of OTA Updates 
To reduce dependency on frequent cloud builds, Over -the-Air (OTA) updates were adopted for 
software modifications that did not require changes to native application code. 
This significantly accelerated development by allowing application improvements to be distributed 
almost immediately without generating entirely new Android application packages. 
Native production builds were reserved for updates requiring native dependencies or platform 
configuration changes. 
5.5.5 Efficient Cloud Resource Utilization 
Cloud resources were carefully managed throughout development to remain within the operational 
limits of the available free service plans. 
Unused resources were removed where appropriate, unnecessary build generation was avoided, 
database queries were optimized, and application assets were managed efficiently to minimize 
bandwidth consumption. 
5.5.6 Documentation Throughout Development 
Comprehensive technical documentation was maintained throughout the software development 
lifecycle using Markdown documentation stored within the project's GitHub repository.

information.

5.6 Limitations of the Developed System 
Although the ACE application successfully satisfies the objectives of this study, certain limitations 
remain. 
The current implementation primarily targets Android devices, with iOS support requiring 
additional platform -specific testing and deployment. Integration with institutional information 
systems such as Learning Management Systems (LMS), university portals, and examination 
management platforms has not yet been implemented. AI -assisted features depend on external 
services and internet connectivity, limiting their functionality in offline environments. In addition, 
cloud-based free-tier services introduce const raints on scalability, availability, storage capacity, 
and build frequency that would need to be addressed before large-scale deployment. 
Operational costs also represent a practical limitation. While the project was implemented largely 
using free development tiers, sustained production use would require paid subscriptions for 
backend hosting, database scaling, build services, monitoring, cu stom domains, and potentially 
application distribution. These recurring expenses should be considered when planning 
institutional or commercial deployment.

(ACE) application was developed as a comprehensive academic management platform capable of 
supporting students throughout their academic journey. 
Unlike conventional reminder or note-taking applications that address isolated aspects of academic 
organization, the developed system integrates multiple academic management services within a
