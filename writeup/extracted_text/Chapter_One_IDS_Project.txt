1.1 Background to the Study
The information and communication technologies are changing fast. This has changed the way companies, governments and schools work in the world. Computer networks are like the road for sharing information. Information and communication technologies (ICT) have made it easier for people to communicate, store data, use cloud computing, and access online services from anywhere in the world. They have also improved speed and convenience in many areas. However, these benefits come with major security challenges. Because ICT continues to grow and evolve, the security risks connected to it also keep increasing.
Today, modern networks are frequently targeted by cybercriminals who look for weaknesses they can exploit. Their goal is often to gain unauthorized access, steal sensitive data, disrupt network operations, or demand money through attacks. As Stallings (2021) explains, these growing threats make network security an important issue that cannot be ignored.
The cyber threat landscape has changed significantly in recent years. Cyberattacks are now more frequent, more organized, and more sophisticated than before. Attackers no longer rely on just one simple method; instead, they combine different techniques to increase their chances of success. Common approaches include scanning systems for open entry points, overwhelming servers with heavy traffic to slow them down or shut them off (denial-of-service attacks), trying to crack weak passwords, spreading malicious software, and attempting to gain higher control within a system after they get initial access.
Schools and universities are especially vulnerable to these threats. This is because they have large numbers of users—students, lecturers, and staff—accessing the network every day. They also allow many different devices to connect, such as laptops, phones, tablets, and public computer systems. With so many users and devices, it becomes harder to manage security consistently, and that creates more opportunities for attackers to exploit weak points. In addition, academic institutions often store valuable information, including personal student records, staff information, research data, and examination materials, which can be attractive to cybercriminals.
Because of these factors, cyberattacks are a serious risk for educational institutions, and the need for strong security measures is very real. Researchers such as García-Teodoro and colleagues have warned about these growing threats since 2019, emphasizing that organizations—especially schools—must take cybersecurity seriously to protect their systems and data (García-Teodoro et al., 2019).
Network security is very important, and tools like firewalls and antivirus software are often the first line of defense. Firewalls help control what traffic is allowed in and out of a network, while antivirus software detects and removes known malicious programs. These tools are useful for blocking many common threats, especially attacks coming from outside the network.
However, they are not always enough on their own. In many cases, they struggle to detect threats that are already inside the network, such as an attacker who gained access through a stolen password or a compromised device. This is a serious issue because internal threats can operate quietly for a long time without being noticed. Traditional security tools may also fail to identify errors in network configuration—such as open ports, weak access controls, or misconfigured permissions—that can create security gaps.
Another challenge is that cybercriminals constantly develop new attack methods. Some attacks are designed specifically to bypass firewalls and antivirus systems, especially when the attack is new or does not match known patterns. Because of this, network security needs more than just basic tools; it also requires continuous monitoring, regular updates, proper configuration, and additional detection methods that can identify unusual activity within the network.
So we need systems that can watch what is happening on our network all the time and catch things as they happen. This is where Network Intrusion Detection Systems come in. Network Intrusion Detection Systems are very important, for keeping our networks safe.
A Network Intrusion Detection System or a Network Intrusion Detection System is like a watchdog that keeps an eye on what's happening on a network all the time. It looks at all the information that is being sent over the network. The Network Intrusion Detection System checks the information to see if anything bad is happening.
A Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS) is used to monitor network traffic and detect activities that should not be happening on a network. Its main job is to identify suspicious behavior early, so security teams can respond before serious damage is done. In practice, NIDS helps improve network security by continuously watching what moves across the network and raising alerts when something looks risky.
	
There are two main types of NIDS approaches:
Signature-based detection: This method checks network traffic for patterns that match known attacks. It works like a “blacklist.” If the system has seen an attack type before and has its signature stored, it can detect it quickly and accurately. The advantage is that it produces fewer false alarms for known threats. The limitation is that it may miss new attacks that do not match any stored signature.
Anomaly-based detection: This method looks for behavior that is unusual compared to normal network activity. Instead of relying on known patterns, it learns what “normal” looks like (for example, typical traffic volume, login behavior, or access patterns) and then flags anything that significantly deviates from that baseline. This makes anomaly detection useful for identifying new or previously unseen attacks. However, it can sometimes raise more false alerts, especially when network behavior changes for legitimate reasons.
Using both methods together provides stronger protection than using only one. Signature-based detection is reliable for known threats, while anomaly-based detection helps catch new or evolving attacks. When combined, they improve detection accuracy and give better situational awareness, because the system can confirm suspicious events from more than one angle and provide a clearer picture of what is happening on the network.
In recent years, open-source intrusion detection systems (IDS) such as Snort and Suricata have become more popular and widely used in the cybersecurity community. One major reason for this is that open-source tools are flexible and transparent. Organizations can see how the tools work, review the code if needed, and adjust configurations to suit their specific network environment. Because these platforms are supported by a large community of developers and security professionals, the detection rules and features are continuously improved, and users can also create or modify rules to match the threats most relevant to their networks.

This makes them a practical option for institutions such as schools, universities, and small organizations that may not have a large budget for cybersecurity tools (Caswell & Roesch, 2020). Even with limited resources, these institutions can still deploy effective monitoring and detection systems.
Open-source IDS platforms are also useful because they provide strong monitoring and alerting capabilities. They log traffic events, detect suspicious activity, and generate alerts when potential attacks are identified. These logs and alerts are valuable for understanding what happened during an incident, tracing the attacker’s actions, and responding quickly. In addition, the detailed information collected by tools like Snort and Suricata supports forensic analysis and helps administrators improve network security over time by identifying weaknesses and updating detection rules.
Using an Intrusion Detection System (IDS) in a university environment is valuable because it helps security teams understand the kinds of threats targeting the network and how to respond to them. A university network is usually large and busy, with many users connecting daily—students, lecturers, researchers, and staff. Because of this, it is not always easy to notice suspicious activity early. An IDS makes this easier by monitoring network traffic, detecting unusual behavior, and sending alerts when something looks like an attack. Over time, the alerts and logs from the IDS also give useful insight into real-world threat patterns and the effectiveness of response strategies.
This is especially important in universities because they host many critical digital services. Since universities depend heavily on these systems to operate smoothly, they must be protected from cyber threats. An IDS supports this protection by helping detect attacks early and providing the information needed to investigate incidents and strengthen security over time.

1.2 Statement of the Problem
Many organizations are using networked systems more than ever, but a lot of them still lack effective tools for monitoring activity in real time. As a result, suspicious behavior and active attacks may go unnoticed, especially when the attackers try to operate quietly. Without real-time visibility, it becomes difficult to detect intrusions early and stop them before damage is done.
A major issue is that many organizations do not deploy an Intrusion Detection System (IDS). An IDS is important because it monitors network traffic, identifies unusual or malicious activity, and sends alerts that allow administrators to respond quickly. It provides visibility into what is happening on the network, which is something basic security tools may not always give.
When an organization operates without an IDS, attackers can remain on the network for a long time without being detected. During this time, they may steal data, disrupt services, or access sensitive information such as user records, passwords, or internal documents. This can lead to serious consequences, including financial loss, downtime, reputational damage, and loss of trust. For this reason, deploying an IDS is a key step in strengthening network security and improving incident response.
Organizations need to have real-time monitoring solutions, like an Intrusion Detection System to protect themselves from attackers and keep their information safe.
Although numerous open-source IDS solutions are readily available, their effectiveness depends heavily on proper configuration, rule customization, and performance tuning. Poorly implemented IDS often generate excessive false positive alerts or fail to detect genuine attacks, reducing trust in the system and discouraging adoption. There is therefore a critical need to implement, test, and evaluate open-source IDS platforms in a structured manner to assess their suitability for real-time network threat detection.

This study will be carried out in a simulated environment, which means it will not fully capture how complex real-world internet traffic can be. The lab setup that will be used in this study will provide a controlled setting for testing, but it will not reflect all the “noise” and unexpected behaviour that normally happens on real networks.
In addition, the study will mainly focus on known traffic patterns and common attack behaviours. It will not cover completely new or unknown attacks (often called zero-day attacks), or highly stealthy attacks that are designed to remain hidden for long periods. These advanced threats may behave differently from the attacks to be simulated in this research and may require more complex detection techniques.

Large-scale experiments involving heavy traffic loads, many simultaneous users, and long-term monitoring will not be carried out. Therefore, the findings of this study will be understood within the limits of a simulated network environment, which will not be the same as real internet traffic in an active production network.
1.7 Operational Definition of Key Terms
Network Traffic: The data (packets) moving across a network between devices, servers, and online services.
Network Security: The policies, tools, and practices used to protect a network and its data from unauthorized access, misuse, disruption, or theft.
Cyberattack: A deliberate attempt to compromise, disrupt, or damage a network/system, often by exploiting vulnerabilities or user errors.
Cybercriminal: An individual or group that uses digital methods to carry out illegal activities such as hacking, stealing data, or extorting money.
Vulnerability: A weakness in a system, configuration, or process that can be exploited by an attacker (e.g., open ports, weak access control).
Denial-of-Service (DoS) Attack: An attack that overwhelms a server/network with heavy traffic to slow it down or make services unavailable.
Port Scanning: The process of checking a device/server for open ports and running services in order to identify possible entry points.
Brute-Force Attack: Repeated login attempts (trial-and-error) used to guess passwords and gain access.
Malware: Harmful software designed to damage systems, steal information, or allow unauthorized control of devices.
Firewall: A security tool that controls incoming and outgoing network traffic based on rules, helping block unauthorized or suspicious connections.
Antivirus Software: A security tool that detects, blocks, and removes known malicious software, mainly using known patterns/signatures.
Intrusion: Any unauthorized or suspicious activity that violates the security of a network/system (e.g., illegal access, malicious scanning, attack traffic).
Intrusion Detection System (IDS): A security system that monitors activity (traffic/events) to detect suspicious or malicious behavior and generate alerts.
Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS): A type of IDS that monitors and analyzes network traffic to detect attacks and unusual behavior in real time.
Host-Based Intrusion Detection System (HIDS): An IDS that runs on individual computers/hosts and monitors activity on that specific machine (not the whole network).
Signature-Based Detection: A detection approach that matches network traffic against known attack patterns (“signatures”). Effective for known threats but weak against new/unknown ones.
Anomaly-Based Detection: A detection approach that learns “normal” network behavior and flags activity that significantly deviates from that baseline. Useful for new threats but can raise more false alerts.
Baseline (Normal Network Behavior): The usual/expected pattern of network activity (traffic volume, login behavior, access patterns) used as a reference for anomaly detection.
Log (Event Log): A recorded history of network events/activities captured by security tools for investigation, tracking, and analysis.
False Positive: An alert raised by the IDS for activity that is actually legitimate (not an attack).
False Negative: A real attack or malicious activity that the IDS fails to detect or alert on.
Detection Effectiveness: How well the IDS correctly identifies real attacks and suspicious behavior during monitoring.
Alert Logging Quality: How clearly, consistently, and usefully an IDS records events and alerts for later analysis and incident investigation.
Configuration: The setup of an IDS and network environment (settings, policies, deployment position, enabled features) that determines how it operates.
Real-Time Monitoring: Continuous observation of network activity as it happens, enabling early detection and quick response to threats.
Open-Source IDS: An intrusion detection system whose source code is publicly available, allowing transparency, flexibility, and community-driven improvements.
Snort: A widely used open-source IDS/IPS platform known for rule-based (signature-driven) detection and flexible configurations.
Suricata: An open-source IDS/IPS platform designed for high-performance traffic analysis, supporting signature-based and advanced inspection features.
Virtualized/Simulated Network Environment: A lab setup built using virtual machines and controlled configurations to imitate a real network for testing IDS behavior safely.
Controlled Cyberattack Simulation: Deliberately generating attack traffic in a lab environment (e.g., scans, brute-force attempts) to test and evaluate IDS detection.
Security Testing Tools: Tools used to simulate attacks and generate test traffic (e.g., port scanners and password-testing tools) for controlled IDS evaluation.

2.2 Concept of Network Security
Network security refers to the policies, practices, and technologies used to protect a network and the data it carries. It focuses on keeping network resources safe from unauthorized access, misuse, and damage. In simple terms, network security acts as a protective layer that prevents unauthorized users from accessing sensitive information, altering data, or disrupting network services.
As organizations rely more on internet-based services and cloud storage, network security has become a major requirement for institutions of all sizes. When systems are connected to the internet, they are exposed to different types of threats, and this makes protection and monitoring necessary. Stallings (2021) explains that the growing use of online services has increased the need for stronger network security measures.
The main goals of network security are often described using the CIA triad:
            Confidentiality: ensuring that sensitive information is only accessible to authorized users.
Integrity: ensuring that data remains accurate and is not altered during storage or transmission.
Availability: ensuring that authorized users can access network services and resources when needed.
If confidentiality, integrity, or availability is compromised, it can lead to serious security breaches, data loss, and operational disruption.
Modern networks are also becoming more complex, which increases the number of possible weak points attackers can target. Common threats include port scanning (probing a network to find open ports and services), denial-of-service attacks (attempting to overload a system so it becomes unavailable), spoofing (pretending to be a trusted device or user), malware infections, and unauthorized access through weak passwords or misconfigured systems. These threats often take advantage of weaknesses in network configuration, software vulnerabilities, and even human errors.
Because of this, effective network security requires more than just one tool or one approach. It depends on both preventive controls (such as firewalls, access control, and encryption) and detective controls (such as intrusion detection and continuous monitoring). When these controls work together, they help reduce the chances of attacks succeeding and also improve the ability to detect and respond quickly when threats occur.
2.3 Overview of Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
An Intrusion Detection System (IDS) is a security mechanism used to monitor network traffic or system activities in order to identify suspicious behaviour and possible attacks. It inspects different sources of information, such as data packets moving across the network, log files, and system events, to determine whether an intrusion attempt or abnormal activity is taking place. When the IDS detects something unusual or matches known attack patterns, it generates an alert for the security personnel responsible for the system.
Unlike preventive tools such as firewalls—which are mainly designed to block unauthorized access—an IDS focuses on detection and visibility. It helps administrators understand what is happening on the network, identify attacks that may have bypassed other controls, and respond quickly before the situation becomes worse. As noted by García-Teodoro et al. (2019), IDS plays a key role in informing security teams about attacks that are occurring or have been attempted, supporting effective monitoring and incident response.
Intrusion Detection System (IDS) technologies are now a key part of modern network security. They help identify threats that may slip past basic defenses like firewalls and antivirus tools. An IDS works by monitoring network traffic and system activity, then looking for signs of suspicious behaviour. This makes it possible to detect both external attacks (such as attempts to break in from outside the network) and internal threats (such as a compromised device, stolen credentials, or malicious actions by an insider).
However, the effectiveness of an IDS is not automatic. How well it performs depends on the detection method being used (for example, signature-based or anomaly-based detection), the architecture of the IDS, and how properly it is configured and maintained. Poor configuration can lead to missed attacks or too many false alerts, which reduces the usefulness of the system.
Intrusion detection is especially important in environments like universities and large company networks, where many users are connected at the same time. These networks handle high volumes of traffic from different devices and services, which can make it difficult to notice attacks early using manual observation. Because activity is constant and diverse, attackers can easily hide within normal traffic if there is no active monitoring.
For this reason, intrusion detection tools are necessary to support continuous monitoring, early threat detection, and timely response. By helping administrators detect suspicious behaviour quickly, an IDS contributes to keeping the network secure, stable, and available for legitimate users.
2.4 Types of Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
Intrusion Detection Systems can be classified based on their deployment location, data source, and operational focus. Understanding these classifications is essential for selecting an IDS solution that aligns with an organization’s network architecture and security requirements. The primary categories include Network-Based IDS (NIDS), Host-Based IDS (HIDS), and Hybrid IDS. Each type offers distinct advantages and limitations depending on the monitoring scope and threat model (Garcia-Teodoro et al., 2019).
2.4.1 Network-Based Intrusion Detection System (NIDS)
A Network-Based Intrusion Detection System or Network-Based Intrusion Detection System, is used to keep an eye on the traffic on a network. It does this by looking at the packets that are moving around on the network. The Network-Based Intrusion Detection System is usually put in places on the network like the gateways or the switches so it can see all the traffic that is going back and forth between devices.
A Network-Based Intrusion Detection System (NIDS) monitors traffic moving across a network by inspecting packet information such as headers and, where possible, payloads. It looks for patterns that may indicate malicious activity—for example, port scanning, attempts to disrupt services (such as denial-of-service activity), or the misuse of network protocols. By analysing these patterns, a NIDS can detect and alert administrators to potential intrusions or suspicious behaviour on the network (Stallings, 2021).
One major advantage of a NIDS is that it provides network-wide monitoring without installing software on every individual computer. This makes it a practical option for large environments, where managing security agents on many devices would be difficult and time-consuming. A NIDS can be placed at strategic points in the network to observe traffic across multiple systems, making it useful for organizations with many users and devices.
However, NIDS also has limitations. It may struggle to inspect encrypted traffic, since encryption hides the payload content from the detection system. In addition, if network traffic is very high, a poorly configured or under-resourced NIDS can experience performance issues, leading to missed detections or delayed alerts. For this reason, proper placement, tuning, and adequate system resources are important to ensure the NIDS remains effective in busy network environments.
2.4.2 Host-Based Intrusion Detection System (HIDS)
Host-Based Intrusion Detection Systems (HIDS) work by monitoring activity on individual machines such as servers and workstations. Instead of focusing mainly on network traffic, a HIDS checks things like system logs, file integrity, user activities, and application behaviour to detect actions that appear suspicious. This makes HIDS useful for identifying threats that may not generate much network traffic, such as insider misuse, unauthorized file changes, or malware operating quietly on a single computer. As noted by Scarfone and Mell (2020), this deeper visibility at the host level helps detect certain attacks that network-based monitoring might miss.
A major strength of HIDS is that it provides detailed information about what is happening inside each system. It can show which files were changed, which accounts were used, what processes were running, and what events occurred before and after suspicious activity. This level of detail supports investigation and incident response.
However, deploying HIDS can require more effort because the software must be installed, configured, and maintained on every device being monitored. In large environments, this can become time-consuming and difficult to manage. Also, if a machine is fully compromised, an attacker may attempt to disable or tamper with the HIDS to avoid detection. Because of these limitations, HIDS is often used alongside a Network-Based Intrusion Detection System (NIDS). When both are combined, organizations gain better coverage: NIDS monitors traffic across the network, while HIDS provides detailed monitoring on each machine, improving overall security.

2.4.3 Hybrid Intrusion Detection System
Hybrid Intrusion Detection Systems combine the strengths of Network-Based IDS (NIDS) and Host-Based IDS (HIDS) to provide more complete security coverage. Instead of relying on only network traffic or only host activity, a hybrid IDS monitors both. This means it can detect threats that appear on the network (such as scanning or suspicious connections) and also threats that show up on individual machines (such as unusual logins, file changes, or suspicious processes).
Because it uses information from two sources, a hybrid IDS can improve detection accuracy. When an alert from the network side matches suspicious behaviour on a host, security teams get stronger evidence that a real attack is happening. This reduces the chances of raising unnecessary alarms and helps limit false positives. It also gives a clearer view of what is going on, which is important for investigating complex attacks that may involve multiple steps.
By combining network and host data, hybrid IDS helps security personnel see the full situation and respond more effectively. García-Teodoro et al. (2019) note that this integrated approach supports better decision-making and strengthens intrusion detection in environments where attackers may try to hide their activities.
2.5 Detection Techniques Used in IDS
An Intrusion Detection System (IDS) is only as effective as the detection techniques it uses. In other words, the ability of an IDS to identify malicious activity depends on the methods it applies to separate normal network behaviour from suspicious or harmful behaviour. These methods help the IDS decide what should be treated as an attack and what is safe.
The most common detection approaches used in IDS are signature-based detection, anomaly-based detection, and hybrid detection.
Each technique has strengths and weaknesses, especially when dealing with evolving cyber threats. This is why selecting the right approach—and tuning it properly—is important for maintaining IDS effectiveness over time.
2.5.1 Signature-Based Detection
Signature-based detection is a method used to identify intrusion attempts by comparing current network traffic or system events to a database of known attack patterns. These patterns, called signatures, are created from previously observed attacks and may include specific byte sequences, suspicious command strings, or behaviour that violates normal protocol rules. When the IDS finds a match between what it is seeing and a stored signature, it flags the activity as malicious. This approach is widely used because it is straightforward and effective for threats that have already been identified.
One major strength of signature-based detection is its high accuracy when dealing with known attacks. Because it relies on clearly defined patterns, it can detect common threats with strong precision and usually produces fewer false alarms compared to other approaches. Caswell and Roesch (2020) explain that signature-based techniques are especially useful for recognizing established attack types quickly and reliably.
However, signature-based systems also have a clear limitation: they depend on predefined signatures. If an attacker uses a new technique that has not been recorded yet—often referred to as a zero-day attack—the IDS may fail to detect it because there is no signature to match. For this reason, signature-based systems must be updated regularly so they can include new signatures as threats evolve. Without frequent updates, the IDS becomes less effective over time and may miss emerging attacks.
2.5.2 Anomaly-Based Detection
Anomaly-based detection focuses on identifying activities that do not look normal within a network or system. It works by first learning what “normal” behaviour looks like—such as typical traffic volume, usual login patterns, and common access times—and then flagging events that strongly deviate from that baseline. In simple terms, once the system understands normal behaviour, it becomes easier to spot unusual patterns that may indicate an attack.
A key advantage of anomaly-based detection is that it can detect new or previously unseen attacks, because it does not depend on predefined signatures. This makes it especially useful for identifying emerging threats or attackers who use new techniques to avoid traditional security tools. García-Teodoro et al. (2019) note that anomaly-based detection is important for early identification of threats that are not yet well known.
However, anomaly-based systems can produce more false positives, mainly because network traffic changes often. Normal changes—such as heavy usage during exams, system updates, new devices joining the network, or a change in user behaviour—can trigger alerts even when there is no actual attack. To reduce these false alerts, anomaly-based IDS tools require careful training, tuning, and regular adjustment of thresholds so the system remains accurate as the network environment changes.
2.5.3 Hybrid Detection Approaches
Hybrid detection approaches combine signature-based and anomaly-based methods to improve intrusion detection. Instead of depending on only one technique, a hybrid IDS checks for known attack patterns (signatures) while also watching for unusual behaviour (anomalies). This gives the system a better chance of detecting both familiar threats and new or evolving attacks that may not yet have signatures.
One clear advantage of hybrid detection is improved accuracy. Signature-based detection helps confirm known attacks with fewer false alarms, while anomaly-based detection helps identify suspicious activity that does not match existing signatures. When these two methods are used together, the IDS can reduce false positives and provide more reliable alerts, because evidence from one method can support or validate the other.
Because of this balance, hybrid IDS approaches are becoming more common in intrusion detection. They offer stronger coverage than individual methods and are better suited for modern networks where threats change quickly. Stallings (2021) notes that hybrid detection is increasingly adopted because it combines the strengths of both detection techniques and addresses the weaknesses that each method has when used alone.
2.6 Open-Source Intrusion Detection Systems
Open-source Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) have become increasingly popular in network security because they offer a strong mix of flexibility, transparency, and low cost. Since the source code is publicly available, users can understand how the system works, verify its behaviour, and adjust it to suit their environment. This openness is a major advantage compared to many commercial IDS solutions, which are often expensive, require paid licenses, and do not allow users to view or modify the internal workings of the system.
With open-source IDS platforms, organizations can customize detection rules, tune alert thresholds, and integrate the tools with their existing network setup. This is important because networks differ in size, traffic patterns, and risk level—so a “one-size-fits-all” configuration rarely works well. Another strength is the active community support behind open-source tools. Security professionals and developers regularly share updates, detection rules, and threat intelligence, which helps improve the systems over time. Caswell and Roesch (2020) highlight that community-driven development plays a key role in keeping these tools effective as threats evolve.
Open-source IDS solutions are especially useful for schools, universities, and organizations with limited budgets. Many institutions cannot afford the high costs of commercial security products, yet they still face real cybersecurity threats. Open-source IDS tools provide affordable monitoring and alerting capabilities similar to those available in larger organizations. They log network activity, generate alerts when suspicious events occur, and provide useful information for investigation and incident response. They also remain adaptable over time because rules and configurations can be updated as new threats emerge.
However, open-source IDS platforms still require proper installation, configuration, and ongoing management. If rules are poorly written, if the IDS is placed in the wrong part of the network, or if it is not tuned to the environment, the system may generate too many false alarms or fail to detect real attacks. This is why understanding how these systems operate internally is important for effective deployment.
Among the most widely used open-source IDS platforms are Snort and Suricata. For this reason, this study focuses specifically on Snort and Suricata, examining how they work, how they are configured, and how well they perform in detecting common network attacks.
2.7 Snort Intrusion Detection System
Snort is one of the earliest and most widely used open-source tools for network intrusion detection. It was created by Martin Roesch in 1998, with the goal of monitoring network traffic in real time and recording packet activity for analysis. Over the years, Snort has grown from a simple packet sniffer into a full intrusion detection and prevention platform, supported by a strong user community. It is also maintained and supported through Cisco’s security ecosystem, including the Talos threat intelligence team (Caswell & Roesch, 2020).
Snort Architecture
Snort is built using a modular design, meaning it has separate components that work together to capture traffic, analyze it, detect suspicious activity, and then report what it finds. This structure makes it easier to extend, tune, and adapt Snort to different network environments. The main components include:
Packet capture
Packet decoding
Preprocessors
Detection engine
Logging and alerting system
Output modules
Each component plays a specific role in helping Snort detect malicious activity accurately and report it quickly.
How Snort Processes Network Traffic
            Packet capture:
Snort captures live network traffic using packet capture libraries such as libpcap. This allows it to observe packets as they move across the network in real time, depending on where Snort is placed (for example, at a gateway or a core switch).
Packet decoding:
After capturing packets, the decoder interprets them based on network protocol formats. It extracts useful details such as source and destination IP addresses, ports, protocol type, flags, and payload structure. This step is important because Snort must correctly understand what each packet represents before it can inspect it properly.
Preprocessors:
Preprocessors prepare traffic for deeper inspection. They can reassemble fragmented packets, normalize traffic, and detect protocol anomalies. This stage improves detection accuracy because many attacks try to confuse detection systems by splitting payloads into fragments or using unusual protocol behaviour. Preprocessors help Snort “clean up” and organize traffic so the next stage—detection—has a clearer view of what is happening.
Detection engine:
This is the core of Snort. The detection engine compares network traffic against a rule set (signatures). These rules define what suspicious or malicious activity looks like—such as port scans, brute-force attempts, exploit patterns, or malware communication. When a rule matches, Snort generates an event and records relevant details so it can be reviewed later. Snort is especially strong at detecting known threats, but it depends on having updated and well-written rules (Caswell & Roesch, 2020).
Logging and alerting:
Once suspicious traffic is detected, Snort can log the event, raise an alert, and store evidence for investigation. This is useful for incident response because it provides a timeline of what happened, what rule was triggered, and what traffic caused the alert.
Output modules:
Output modules control how Snort reports results. Depending on configuration, alerts can be written to files, sent to a console, exported to databases, or integrated with other monitoring tools such as SIEM platforms. This flexibility is one reason Snort fits into different security setups.
Strengths and Limitations
One major strength of Snort is its mature rule ecosystem. Because it has been around for a long time, it benefits from extensive community support and frequent rule updates, which improves detection for common and well-known attacks (Caswell & Roesch, 2020).
However, Snort can face performance challenges in high-speed networks, especially in older versions that relied heavily on single-threaded processing. In environments with very heavy traffic, this can affect packet processing efficiency and may lead to dropped packets if the IDS is not properly tuned or deployed with sufficient resources. For this reason, careful placement, hardware planning, and rule optimization are important when deploying Snort on busy networks.
2.8 Suricata Intrusion Detection System
Suricata is an open-source Network Intrusion Detection and Prevention System (NIDS/NIPS) developed and supported by the Open Information Security Foundation (OISF). It was created to address some of the limitations found in older IDS tools, especially around speed, scalability, and deeper traffic inspection. Because Suricata is open-source, organizations can deploy it without licensing costs, review how it works, and adapt it to suit their own network environment.
A major strength of Suricata is its focus on high-performance traffic analysis. The engine is built on a multi-threaded and highly scalable code base, which allows it to take advantage of modern multi-core processors and inspect multi-gigabit traffic more effectively. This makes it well suited for high-bandwidth environments where the IDS must keep up with heavy traffic without losing visibility.
Suricata also stands out because of its deep protocol inspection and application-layer awareness. It supports automatic protocol detection and can recognize protocols such as HTTP, DNS, FTP, SMTP, SSH, and TLS—even when they run on non-standard ports. This is useful because attackers often try to hide traffic by moving it to unusual ports. With Suricata’s approach, the correct detection and logging logic can still be applied once the protocol is identified.
In addition to intrusion detection, Suricata is often described as “more than an IDS” because it provides strong network security monitoring (NSM) features. It can log HTTP activity, record DNS queries and responses, store TLS certificate details, and even extract files from network flows for further investigation. This makes it useful not only for detection, but also for incident response and forensic analysis, since analysts can review richer context around suspicious events.
Another practical advantage is rule support. Suricata implements a full signature language for matching known threats and policy violations, and it is widely used with community and vendor-supported rulesets. This makes it easier for organizations to build and maintain detection coverage without starting from scratch.
Even with these strengths, Suricata is not “set-and-forget.” Its deeper inspection features and high-speed processing can demand significant CPU and memory, especially on busy networks. If it is poorly tuned—such as running too many heavy rules, using weak capture settings, or deploying it on underpowered hardware—it can lead to performance issues or noisy alerts. Because of this, effective use of Suricata depends on proper deployment planning, rule tuning, and regular maintenance to keep detection accurate and reliable.
2.9 Conceptual Comparison of Snort and Suricata
Snort and Suricata are two widely used open-source IDS platforms, and both are effective, but they are built differently. Those design differences affect how they perform and the kind of environments they fit best.
Snort is generally lighter and easier to deploy. It has been around for a long time, so it benefits from a large rule ecosystem and strong community support. Because it is less demanding in many setups, Snort is often a practical choice for smaller networks, schools, and organizations that want a reliable IDS without needing high-end hardware or complex tuning.
Suricata, on the other hand, is designed with modern high-traffic networks in mind. It supports multi-threading, which allows it to process large amounts of traffic more efficiently on multi-core systems. It also offers deeper protocol inspection and stronger visibility at the application layer, which can be useful in large organizations or networks where traffic is heavy and threats are more complex.
Choosing between Snort and Suricata depends on several factors, not just “which one is better.” Important considerations include the size of the network, average and peak traffic volume, available system resources, and the skill level of the administrators managing the IDS. Snort may be easier to maintain in a smaller environment, while Suricata may be better suited for high-speed networks that require stronger performance and deeper inspection. In the end, understanding what each tool is best at helps an organization select an IDS that matches its security needs and what it can realistically support.

2.11 Research Gap
From the studies reviewed, it is clear that a lot of progress has been made in the development and evaluation of Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS). Researchers have proposed different architectures, detection techniques, and evaluation methods, and the field has grown beyond basic rule matching into more advanced approaches. However, there are still important gaps. Many existing studies focus heavily on the theoretical side of IDS, or they evaluate performance using only one angle—such as detection rate—without considering other practical factors like false positives, alert quality, resource usage, and how the IDS behaves under realistic traffic conditions. In some cases, the evaluations are not consistent enough to allow a meaningful comparison across different IDS platforms.
Another major limitation is that relatively few studies examine how open-source IDS tools perform in education-focused environments. University networks are unique because they support many users, devices, and services at the same time, and they often operate with limited cybersecurity budgets. Yet, there is still limited research that tests open-source IDS solutions under conditions that reflect school networks, including realistic traffic and controlled attack simulations running concurrently.
This study responds to these gaps by implementing and comparatively analysing open-source IDS platforms in a virtualized network environment. It focuses on practical, real-time intrusion detection by simulating common attacks while monitoring normal traffic, and it evaluates performance using clear operational measures such as detection accuracy, false positive rate, responsiveness, and overall efficiency. This helps provide a more balanced and realistic understanding of how open-source IDS tools perform in conditions similar to those found in academic networks.

METHODOLOGY
3.1 Introduction
This section describes the methodology for developing and evaluating an open-source Network Intrusion Detection System (NIDS) for real-time threat detection. It explains the system design, tools used, and how the NIDS will operate within a controlled test environment. It also outlines the data sources, attack simulation procedures, traffic capture methods, and performance metrics used to assess effectiveness. Overall, the approach is structured, transparent, and repeatable to support reliable evaluation and reproducible results.
3.2 Research Design
This study will use a system implementation and experimental research design. First, a working model of the Intrusion Detection System (IDS) will be developed and will be deployed in a controlled environment. The environment will be designed so that key conditions—such as network setup, traffic flow, and attack scenarios—can be managed and repeated. After deployment, different cyberattacks will be simulated against the network to test how well the IDS will detect them and how it will respond.
An experimental approach will be suitable for cybersecurity research because it will allow systems to be tested under controlled conditions where results can be observed and measured directly. Instead of relying only on theory, the researcher will be able to design test cases, run them, and record how the system will behave. This will make it easier to evaluate performance fairly and consistently. As Stallings (2021) will explain, experimental designs will be useful in cybersecurity because they will help researchers study real system behaviour while controlling the variables that could affect results.
This study will be focused on practical evaluation, not just discussion of concepts. By placing the IDS in a test environment and running controlled attack simulations, the research will connect network security ideas to real events. This will make it possible to assess how effective the IDS will be in practice, including how reliable its alerts will be, how quickly it will respond to suspicious activity, and how well it will perform under different network and attack conditions.
This study will use a combination of software tools and virtual hardware to design, deploy, and test the Intrusion Detection System (IDS) in a controlled environment. Instead of using a live network, the experiment will be carried out in a simulated setup so testing can be done safely without affecting real users or production systems.
Virtualization Platform (VirtualBox):	
Oracle VirtualBox will be used to create and run multiple virtual machines (VMs) on a single physical computer. Each VM will be assigned a specific role in the network, making it possible to build a small test network that will behave like a real one while still remaining fully controlled.
Intrusion Detection Platforms (Snort and Suricata):
The IDS platforms that will be used for detection will be Snort and Suricata. These tools will be selected because they are widely adopted open-source solutions, they have strong community support, and there will be extensive documentation available to guide configuration, rule writing, and performance evaluation.
Attack Simulation Environment (Kali Linux):
A Kali Linux virtual machine will be used as the attacker system. Kali Linux will include common security testing tools such as Nmap for scanning and Metasploit for exploitation. These tools will be used to simulate controlled attacks against the virtual network so the IDS response can be evaluated.
Monitoring System (Ubuntu Linux):
An Ubuntu Linux virtual machine will be used as the IDS monitoring host. Ubuntu will be chosen because it is stable, widely supported, and compatible with both Snort and Suricata. It will provide a reliable environment for installing, configuring, and running the IDS tools during testing.
3.4 System Architecture
The test environment will be set up to represent a small network with three main systems: an attacker machine, a target machine, and an intrusion detection system (IDS). All three systems will be connected on the same virtual network, which will make it possible to control traffic flow and run attacks safely without affecting any real network.
The IDS machine will be configured to capture and inspect all traffic moving across the network. It will be placed in a monitoring setup that will allow it to see every packet transmitted between the attacker and the target, ensuring full visibility of network activity during testing.
In this setup, the attacker node will generate malicious traffic using reconnaissance and exploitation tools, while the target node will act as a vulnerable system running network services. The IDS will continuously monitor the traffic between both nodes, will analyze packets in real time, and will raise alerts whenever suspicious behaviour is detected. This design will make it easier to observe how the IDS will react during attack scenarios and will provide a realistic way to evaluate detection performance under controlled conditions.
3.5 IDS Configuration and Rule Implementation
The IDS platforms will be configured using a mix of default/community rule sets and custom rules to be created for this study. The default rules will help detect common, well-known threats, while the custom rules will be written to focus on specific activities to be tested in the lab—such as port scanning and unauthorized access attempts. Using both rule types will make the IDS more flexible and will improve its ability to detect different kinds of attacks during the experiment.
The custom rules will be developed based on the attack scenarios to be simulated during testing. Each rule will specify what the IDS should look for, including the protocol involved, the source and destination addresses or ports, and specific patterns within the traffic. After writing the rules, they will be adjusted and fine-tuned to reduce unnecessary alerts. This will be important because poorly tuned rules can generate too many warnings, making it harder to identify real threats. By tuning the rule set, the IDS will produce alerts that will be more meaningful and relevant.
Overall, combining default rules with custom rules will allow the IDS to detect both general threats and the targeted attacks to be designed for the test environment. This approach will improve detection coverage and will provide a clearer picture of how well the IDS platforms will respond to different intrusion attempts.
3.6 Attack Simulation Procedure
To evaluate how well the Intrusion Detection System (IDS) will perform, a series of controlled tests will be carried out in the virtual network. The attacker machine will begin with basic reconnaissance activities, including ping checks and connection probing, and then will use Nmap to scan the target for open ports and running services. After reconnaissance, additional attack activities will be simulated, including repeated login attempts to imitate brute-force behaviour and the transmission of suspicious or malformed packets to trigger detection rules. These steps will be used to observe how quickly and accurately the IDS will detect common network threats.
Each attack scenario will be repeated multiple times to ensure the results will be consistent and not based on a one-time occurrence. During every test, the IDS will log events and will generate alerts whenever it detects suspicious activity. These alerts and log entries will be recorded and will be organized for later review so that detection patterns can be compared across different trials and across the two IDS platforms.
In addition to malicious traffic, normal and harmless network activity will also be generated. This will be done to check whether the IDS will incorrectly flag legitimate traffic as malicious and to measure how sensitive the system will be under typical network conditions. Including both attack traffic and normal traffic will make the evaluation more realistic and will help assess not only detection capability, but also the false positive behaviour of the IDS.
3.7 Data Collection
The data collected from the experiment will come mainly from two sources: IDS alert logs and packet capture (PCAP) files. The alert logs will provide detailed records of detected events, including the time each alert will occur, the type of suspected attack, the source and destination IP addresses, and the specific rule or signature that will trigger the alert. This information will make it possible to track what the IDS will detect and how it will classify each activity.
The PCAP files will be used to confirm and validate the IDS results. By reviewing captured packets, the study will be able to verify whether an alert will match actual malicious traffic and also understand the traffic pattern that will lead to detection. In this way, packet captures will serve as supporting evidence, helping to confirm the accuracy of the IDS logs and reduce the chance of relying on false or misleading alerts.
Overall, combining IDS logs with packet capture analysis will provide a clearer picture of network activity during testing and will strengthen the reliability of the evaluation results.
3.8 Performance Evaluation Metrics
The IDS will be evaluated to determine how effective it will be during the experiments. The assessment will focus on how accurately the system will detect attacks, how often it will raise incorrect alerts, and how reliably it will generate and record alerts during different test scenarios. These measures will help show whether the IDS can be trusted to detect real threats without overwhelming administrators with unnecessary warnings.
To evaluate performance, the study will use the following metrics:
I. Detection accuracy: This will refer to the proportion of simulated attacks that the IDS will correctly identify as malicious. A higher accuracy will suggest the IDS will be effective at recognizing the attacks it is meant to detect.
II. False positive rate: This will occur when the IDS will flag normal, harmless activity as an attack. High false positives will reduce trust in the system and can lead to alert fatigue.
III. False negative rate: This will happen when the IDS will fail to detect a real attack. False negatives will be especially serious because they will mean threats can pass through unnoticed.
IV. Alert generation efficiency: This will measure how well the IDS will produce useful and timely alerts during an attack. It will consider whether alerts will be generated quickly, clearly, and consistently enough to support investigation and response.
Together, these metrics will provide a clear picture of IDS effectiveness by showing both its ability to detect attacks and its tendency to generate errors. As reported by García-Teodoro et al. (2019), using measures such as accuracy, false positives, false negatives, and alert efficiency will help evaluate how reliable an intrusion detection system will be in practical network conditions.
3.9 Ethical Considerations
All attack simulations will be carried out in a fully isolated virtual environment to ensure they will not affect any external or live networks. The experiments will not involve real user data; only test data and controlled traffic will be used throughout the study. This approach will support ethical research practices by protecting privacy and preventing unintended harm.
In addition, the study will follow responsible cybersecurity research principles by restricting all testing activities to systems that will be set up and authorized for experimentation. No attacks will be directed at real-world targets, and all tools will be used strictly for controlled evaluation within the approved lab environment.
